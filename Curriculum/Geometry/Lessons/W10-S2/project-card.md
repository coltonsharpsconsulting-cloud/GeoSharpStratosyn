# Area Hunt & Build

*Find real surfaces that aren't rectangles — a pennant, a tabletop, a tile — measure each one's base and true perpendicular height, and compute its exact area. Then flip the proof around: cut shapes from grid paper and confirm every formula by counting squares.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a measurement log (three surfaces, one of each shape, with area computed) plus your grid-paper cutouts or photos, for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A tape measure or ruler, a pencil, and paper
- A set-square, a book's straight edge, or a large index card (for finding a TRUE perpendicular height, not a guessed slant)
- Grid paper, scissors, and glue or tape (for the Grid-Paper Confirm path)
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo
- Three real "not-a-rectangle" surfaces to measure — pick from: a triangular pennant or flag, a trapezoidal tabletop or lampshade panel, a parallelogram-shaped floor tile or book propped at an angle, a roof gable seen from the side, a slice of pizza or sandwich

## Part 1 — Real-Surface Hunt (measure real areas)
1. Find three different surfaces in the real world — one that's roughly a **parallelogram**, one that's roughly a **triangle**, and one that's roughly a **trapezoid**.
2. For each one, measure the **base** (any one straight side) and then find the **true perpendicular height** — hold your set-square, or the square corner of a book, against the base and slide it up until it touches the opposite side or point. That's the height; the slanted side is NOT the height.
3. For the trapezoid, measure **both** parallel bases.
4. Record the shape, its base(s) and height, the formula you used, and the computed area, for all three.

## Part 2 — Grid-Paper Confirm (prove the formulas by counting)
5. Cut a parallelogram, a triangle, and a trapezoid out of grid paper, each with whole-number base and height.
6. Count full squares plus half-squares to find each shape's area by counting alone.
7. Compute the same area with the correct formula and compare — they should match. If they don't, recheck which side you measured as the height.

## Composite bonus
Measure or sketch one real composite surface (an L-shaped room, a pennant with a rectangular tail flying behind it) and find its total area by splitting it into simpler shapes and adding the parts.

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch a parallelogram, a triangle, and a trapezoid with dimensioned base and height constraints, then use the measurement tool to read each area back and confirm it matches your hand computation. Export a screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Tape measures can snap back — retract slowly, not with a flick. Scissors have points — cut away from your hand. Don't climb or stretch to measure anything overhead or unstable (a roofline, a high shelf) — measure from the ground, or ask an adult for help.

## Turn in (rubric — 7 pts)
| Criterion | Pts |
|---|---|
| Three real surfaces measured (base + true perpendicular height, both bases for the trapezoid) | 2 |
| Area computed correctly for all three, using the correct formula for each shape | 2 |
| At least one answer confirmed a second way (grid-square count or CAD measurement tool) | 1 |
| Every measurement and shape clearly labeled | 1 |
| Photo of measurement log and/or grid-paper cutouts | 1 |

Photograph your measurement log and drop it in the [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]]. In Session 3 you'll defend, in words, why a triangle is always exactly half of a same-base-same-height rectangle and why a trapezoid needs the average of its two bases — then take the checkpoint.
