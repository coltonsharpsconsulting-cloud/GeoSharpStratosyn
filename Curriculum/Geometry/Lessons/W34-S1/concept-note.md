# The Six Trigonometric Ratios

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a right triangle and a pencil in hand, labeling opposite, adjacent, and hypotenuse as each ratio comes up. Two lessons ago you met SOHCAHTOA. This week those three ratios get their three missing partners, and the six finally stand together as one family.*

## The one big question

Sine, cosine, and tangent each compare two sides of a right triangle. What if you just flip the fraction?

That single move — turning opp/hyp upside down into hyp/opp — is the entire lesson. Flip all three SOHCAHTOA ratios and you get the three you're missing: **cosecant, secant, and cotangent.**

## The three you know, and their reciprocal partners

Every trig ratio compares two sides of a right triangle *relative to θ* — the **opposite** leg (across from θ), the **adjacent** leg (next to θ, not the hypotenuse), and the **hypotenuse** (across from the right angle). Reciprocal just means "flip the fraction: swap top and bottom."

| Ratio | Definition | Reciprocal | Definition |
|---|---|---|---|
| **sin θ** | $\dfrac{\text{opp}}{\text{hyp}}$ | **csc θ** (cosecant) | $\dfrac{\text{hyp}}{\text{opp}}$ |
| **cos θ** | $\dfrac{\text{adj}}{\text{hyp}}$ | **sec θ** (secant) | $\dfrac{\text{hyp}}{\text{adj}}$ |
| **tan θ** | $\dfrac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}}$ | **cot θ** (cotangent) | $\dfrac{\text{adj}}{\text{opp}}$ |

Read that table by rows: **csc is sin flipped, sec is cos flipped, cot is tan flipped.** Nothing new to memorize about *which sides* go where — you already own that from SOHCAHTOA. You're only learning to write the same fraction upside down and give it a new name.

- **Cosecant (csc θ)** — the reciprocal of sine: $\csc\theta = \dfrac{1}{\sin\theta} = \dfrac{\text{hyp}}{\text{opp}}$
- **Secant (sec θ)** — the reciprocal of cosine: $\sec\theta = \dfrac{1}{\cos\theta} = \dfrac{\text{hyp}}{\text{adj}}$
- **Cotangent (cot θ)** — the reciprocal of tangent: $\cot\theta = \dfrac{1}{\tan\theta} = \dfrac{\text{adj}}{\text{opp}}$

## Finding all six from one triangle

Say a right triangle has legs 6 and 8 with hypotenuse 10, and θ sits at the vertex between the leg of 8 and the hypotenuse (so the leg of 6 is opposite θ, and the leg of 8 is adjacent to θ):

$$\sin\theta=\frac{6}{10}=\frac{3}{5} \qquad \cos\theta=\frac{8}{10}=\frac{4}{5} \qquad \tan\theta=\frac{6}{8}=\frac{3}{4}$$
$$\csc\theta=\frac{10}{6}=\frac{5}{3} \qquad \sec\theta=\frac{10}{8}=\frac{5}{4} \qquad \cot\theta=\frac{8}{6}=\frac{4}{3}$$

Notice each ratio on the bottom row is just the top row's matching fraction flipped — and **always reduce if possible** (10/6 is not fully simplified; 5/3 is). Math-U-See asks for every one of the six, written in reduced fraction form, from a single labeled triangle. That's the whole lesson in one worked example.

## The identity hiding underneath

Because $\sin\theta = \frac{6}{10}$ and $\cos\theta = \frac{8}{10}$, square each and add them: $\left(\frac{6}{10}\right)^2+\left(\frac{8}{10}\right)^2=\frac{36}{100}+\frac{64}{100}=\frac{100}{100}=1$.

That's not a coincidence — it's the Pythagorean theorem ($a^2+b^2=c^2$) in disguise. Divide both sides by $c^2$ and you get exactly:

$$\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$$

Math-U-See calls this **"the Pythagorean theorem expressed with trig ratios."** It's true for *every* right triangle, no matter how the legs are labeled or how big θ is, because it comes straight from $a^2+b^2=c^2$.

## Watch it work

**Flip and check.** Take any triangle's sin θ and csc θ. Multiply them: $\frac{6}{10}\times\frac{10}{6}=1$. Reciprocals always multiply to exactly 1 — that's a fast way to check your work on any pair.

**The vertex moves, the labels don't stay put.** Rotate the same triangle so θ sits at the *other* acute vertex. Suddenly the leg that was opposite is now adjacent, and vice versa — sin θ and cos θ swap values. What never changes is the *rule*: opposite is always the leg across from θ, adjacent is always the leg touching θ that isn't the hypotenuse. Math-U-See tests this directly by rotating the triangle between problems on the same worksheet.

## Why this matters

A carpenter reading a roof pitch, a surveyor sighting a hilltop, a pilot's glide-slope angle — every one of them is someone using one trig ratio to find a side or angle they couldn't reach with a tape measure. This week you'll **build and measure** a real right triangle end to end — including using trig to find a height you can't climb up and measure directly — then **prove** you can defend the reciprocal relationships and pass the mastery checkpoint. Lesson 30 is the last of the thirty — master these six ratios, and you're ready for the year-end final.
