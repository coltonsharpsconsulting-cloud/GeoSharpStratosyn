# Angle Type Hunt & Reflex Build

*Find one real example each of an acute, right, obtuse, and straight angle hiding in your everyday world — then build the one type you can't just stumble across: a reflex angle, by rotating a hinge or a clock's hands past a straight line.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a hunt log (four measured real-world angles, one per type) plus a reflex build with its measurement, photographed for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A protractor (standard clear plastic, reads 0°–180°)
- A ruler, a sharp pencil, and blank or grid paper
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo
- **For the reflex build**, one of:
  - A real analog clock (or a clock app with a second hand you can pause), **or**
  - Two straight strips (paint stirrers, rulers, or stiff cardboard) joined at one end with a brass paper fastener, so they swing freely a full 360°

## Part 1 — Angle Type Hunt (measure four real angles)
1. Find one real angle for each of the first four types, each with a clear **vertex** (the pivot point) and two straight **sides**:
   - **Acute** — a pair of scissors barely open, a door cracked just a little, a partly folded piece of paper.
   - **Right** — a book's cover square to its spine, a picture frame's corner, a step stool's leg.
   - **Obtuse** — a laptop screen tipped back, a reclined chair back, a wide-open door.
   - **Straight** — a book lying open completely flat, a yardstick against a wall with no bend, a hinge folded all the way back on itself.
2. For each one, hold your protractor's center crosshair right at the vertex, line up the **0° line along one side**, and read where the other side crosses the scale — to the nearest whole degree.
3. Record what the object was and your measured degree, for all four — and double-check each measurement actually matches the type you claimed.

## Part 2 — Reflex Build (build the angle you can't just find lying around)
A reflex angle is bigger than a straight line — you have to rotate **past** 180° to make one, which is why you won't stumble across one sitting still the way you can a right angle.

4. **Using a clock:** pick a time where the hands are far apart (like 10:00 or 4:20). Measure the *smaller* angle between the hands first. Then measure the angle **the long way around** — going the other direction from one hand to the other, past the 12 or past whichever side is longer. That long-way-around measurement is your reflex angle.
5. **Using a hinge:** open your two strips to any angle less than 180°, and measure it. Then keep rotating one strip in the *same* direction until it's swept all the way around past where the other strip started — stop, and measure that larger sweep. That's your reflex angle.
6. **Check the math.** Add your smaller angle and your reflex angle together. They should land on exactly 360° — one full turn around the vertex. If they don't, re-measure.

## Safety
Protractors and pencils have points — carry them capped or pointed down. Paper fasteners (brads) have sharp prongs on the back — press them flat and keep fingers clear. If you're measuring a real door or laptop hinge, don't force it past its natural range of motion.
