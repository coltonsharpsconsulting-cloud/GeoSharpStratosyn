# Circle Hunt & the π Experiment

*Find three real circles of clearly different sizes — a can lid, a bike wheel, a plate. Measure each one's diameter with a ruler and its circumference by wrapping a string around it. Then divide circumference by diameter and watch the same constant, π, appear every time — no matter how big or small the circle is.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a measurement log (three circles, diameter + circumference + C÷d + computed area for each) plus a photo, for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A tape measure or ruler, a pencil, and paper
- A length of string or yarn (long enough to wrap around your largest object) and scissors
- A calculator
- Three real circular objects of clearly **different sizes** — pick from: a can lid or jar lid, a bike wheel or tire, a dinner plate, a clock face, a coin, a Frisbee, a round table
- (Optional) one elliptical / oval object — an oval mirror, an egg-shaped platter, an oval rug
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — The π Experiment (discover the constant)
1. For each of your three circles, measure the **diameter** — straight across, through the center — with a ruler or tape measure.
2. Wrap the string once around the object at its **widest point** (that's the circumference), mark or pinch where it meets itself, then lay the string flat and measure its length with the ruler. That length is your circumference.
3. Divide: **C ÷ d**. Record the result for each object.
4. Compare your three results. They should all land close to **3.14** — the same constant, whether the circle is tiny or huge.

## Part 2 — Confirm with the Formulas
5. Using the diameter you measured (half of it is the radius), compute each circle's circumference with **C = πd** and compare it to the circumference you measured directly with the string. How close are they?
6. Compute each circle's area with **A = πr²**.

## Ellipse bonus
If you measured an oval object, find its long axis and short axis, then compute its area with **A = π · (½ long axis) · (½ short axis)**.

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch a circle with a dimensioned radius constraint, then use the measurement tool to read back the circumference and area and confirm they match your hand computation. Export a screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Scissors have points — cut away from your hand. Tape measures can snap back — retract slowly, not with a flick. If a wheel or object is attached to something (a bike, a vehicle), don't unmount it — measure it in place.

| C ÷ d computed correctly for all three, each landing close to π (3.14) | 2 |
