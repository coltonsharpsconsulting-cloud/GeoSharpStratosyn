# Force the Triangle Lab

*Yesterday you argued that two angles and one side should force exactly one triangle. Today you build it with your own hands — twice — and check whether "forced" really means "no other triangle possible." Then you build from angles alone, with no side at all, and watch that guarantee disappear.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** an ASA build, an AAS build, and an AAA (angles-only) build, each photographed with your measurements, for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- Cardstock or heavy paper (at least 8.5"×11", a few sheets)
- A ruler marked in a consistent unit (inches or cm)
- A protractor
- A pencil and a fine-tip marker
- Scissors
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — ASA Build (Included Side)
1. Draw a baseline segment $\overline{AB}$ of a length you choose (6 in works well). This is your **included side**.
2. Pick two angle measures (e.g., 50° and 65°) that sum to less than 180°. At point A, use your protractor to draw a ray at your first angle above the baseline. At point B, draw a ray at your second angle above the baseline, tilting the other way.
3. Extend both rays until they cross. Mark that crossing point C and connect the triangle.
4. **Rebuild it.** On a fresh sheet, using the exact same $\overline{AB}$ length and the exact same two angles, build the triangle again from scratch. Cut both out and lay one on top of the other.

## Part 2 — AAS Build (Non-Included Side)
5. This time you're handed $\angle A$, $\angle B$, and side $\overline{AC}$ — a side that only touches $\angle A$, not $\angle B$. Here's the trick: compute $\angle C = 180° - \angle A - \angle B$ first. Once you know $\angle C$, side $\overline{AC}$ sits **between** $\angle A$ and $\angle C$ — so draw it exactly like an ASA build, using $\overline{AC}$ as your baseline and $\angle A$, $\angle C$ at its two ends instead.
6. Extend the two rays until they cross at B, and connect the triangle.

## Part 3 — AAA Build (Angles Only, No Side)
7. Using the SAME two angles from Part 1, draw a NEW baseline of a completely different length — your choice, no side was given to you this time.
8. Build the triangle the same way (protractor at each end, extend to meet).
9. Compare this triangle to your Part 1 triangle: measure all three sides of both. Are the angles the same? Are the sides the same?

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch a triangle with two angle constraints and one length constraint (ASA or AAS), then try deleting the length constraint and dragging a vertex — watch the sketch go from fully constrained (one triangle) to underconstrained (many triangles, same angles).

## Safety
Scissors pointed away from fingers and closed when not in use. Cut on a stable, flat surface.
