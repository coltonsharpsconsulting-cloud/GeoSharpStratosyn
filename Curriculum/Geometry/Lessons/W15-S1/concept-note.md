# Circumference & Area of Circles

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a pencil, a coin or a lid to trace, and a calculator in hand. Lesson 12 gave you the round shape's vocabulary — radius, diameter, chord, arc. This week those two measurements, radius and diameter, turn into two exact numbers: the distance around a circle, and the space inside it.*

## The one big question

Every circle you'll ever meet — a coin, a manhole cover, a bike wheel, a plate — has a distance around it (its circumference) and a distance across it (its diameter). Divide the first by the second and you get… the same number, every single time, no matter how big or small the circle is. What is that constant, and how does it unlock both the circumference and the area?

## Radius, diameter & the constant that never changes

| Term | What it is | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|
| **Radius (r)** | The distance from the center to any point on the circle. | Half of the diameter — always. |
| **Diameter (d)** | The distance straight across the circle, through the center. | Twice the radius: $d = 2r$. Mixing up radius and diameter is the single most common circle mistake. |
| **π (pi)** | The constant ratio of any circle's circumference to its diameter: $\pi = C \div d$. | It's the *same number* for a coin and for a bike wheel. Math-U-See uses the decimal **π ≈ 3.14**, and the fraction **π ≈ 22/7** when the radius or diameter is a multiple of 7 (it divides out evenly). |

## Two formulas, one constant

- **Circumference** — the distance *around* a circle: $C = \pi d = 2\pi r$. Both forms are the same formula; since $d = 2r$, substituting one for the other changes nothing.
- **Area** — the space *inside* a circle: $A = \pi r^2$. Notice circumference uses $r$ once, but area uses $r$ **squared** — one measures a length, the other measures a surface.

## Watch it work

**Circumference, fractional π.** A circle has a radius of 7 m (Math-U-See's own example — a multiple of 7, so the fraction divides out clean). Using $\pi \approx \tfrac{22}{7}$: $C = 2\pi r = 2 \times \tfrac{22}{7} \times 7 = 44$ m.

**Area, fractional π.** That same circle's area: $A = \pi r^2 = \tfrac{22}{7} \times 7^2 = \tfrac{22}{7} \times 49 = 154$ m².

**Circumference & area, decimal π.** A circle has a diameter of 10 in. $C = \pi d = 3.14 \times 10 = 31.4$ in. Its radius is 5 in, so $A = \pi r^2 = 3.14 \times 25 = 78.5$ in².

## Beyond the circle: the ellipse

An **ellipse** is a stretched circle — it has a long axis and a short axis instead of one radius in every direction. Math-U-See's rule: *multiply one-half the long axis times one-half the short axis times π.* Call those two half-axes $a$ and $b$: $A = \pi a b$. A rug with a long axis of 12 ft and a short axis of 8 ft has $a = 6$ ft and $b = 4$ ft, so $A = \pi(6)(4) = 24\pi \approx 75.36$ ft². Notice that if the long and short axes were equal ($a = b = r$), this formula collapses into the plain circle formula, $A = \pi r^2$ — a circle is just an ellipse whose two axes happen to match.

## Circles wrap the globe: latitude & longitude

The earth is a sphere, and every flat slice through it is a circle. Math-U-See uses exactly this idea to locate any point on earth:

- **Latitude** — circles parallel to the **equator** (latitude 0°), measuring how far a point is **north or south**. The North and South Poles sit at 90°.
- **Longitude** — circles (technically half-circles, pole to pole) that pass through the **North and South Poles**, measuring how far a point is **east or west** of the **prime meridian** (0°), which runs through Greenwich, England.
- Both are measured in **degrees**, and each degree splits further into **60 minutes**, and each minute into **60 seconds** — the same base-60 system a clock uses.
- Give both numbers — a latitude and a longitude — and you've pinpointed one exact location on the globe, the same way an ordered pair pinpoints a point on a graph.

## Why this matters

A can lid, a bike wheel, a pizza, a Ferris wheel, a globe itself — circles are everywhere, and every one of them obeys the same two formulas, tied together by the one constant that never changes. This week you'll **measure** real circles and *discover* π for yourself by wrapping a string around one and dividing, and **prove**, in your own words, why that ratio can never change no matter how big or small the circle gets. Master circumference and area now, and volume and surface area of cylinders, cones, and spheres — coming in the next few lessons — build directly on top of them.
