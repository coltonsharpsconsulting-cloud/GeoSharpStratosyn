# Points, Lines, Rays & Segments

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a pencil in hand, drawing each figure as it comes up. Everything we build this week grows out of these four ideas.*

## The one big question

How little does it take to say *exactly* where something is?

The answer geometry gives is surprising: it takes something with **no size at all**. That's where we start.

## The four building blocks

Every figure you will ever draw in this course is made from four things. They differ by one simple count — **how many endpoints they have.**

| Figure | Endpoints | What it is | How you write it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Point** | — | A single location. No length, no width — just *where*. | A capital letter: point **A** |
| **Line** | none | Straight, runs forever **both** ways. | $\overleftrightarrow{AB}$ (arrows both ends) |
| **Ray** | one | Starts at an endpoint, runs forever **one** way. | $\overrightarrow{AB}$ — endpoint first |
| **Segment** | two | A finite, **measurable** piece of a line. | $\overline{AB}$ (bar, no arrows) |

Read that table twice. The whole lesson is in the **Endpoints** column.

- A **point** marks a spot. It has no size, yet everything is built from it — a line is nothing but an infinite string of points.
- A **line** is perfectly straight and never stops in either direction. Two arrowheads say "keeps going."
- A **ray** is "half a line": it has a starting point (its **endpoint**) and shoots off forever one way — like a beam from a flashlight. **Write the endpoint first:** $\overrightarrow{AB}$ starts at A and goes through B. $\overrightarrow{BA}$ is a *different* ray, pointing the opposite way.
- A **segment** is the part of a line *between* two endpoints. Because it stops at both ends, you can **measure** it — that's what makes segments the workhorses of building and design.

> **The endpoint test.** Ask "how many ends stop?" → 0 ends stop = **line**; 1 end stops = **ray**; 2 ends stop = **segment**. A point isn't on this scale at all — it has no length to stop.

## A few words that describe how figures relate

Math-U-See's Lesson 1 also gives you four comparison words. They're worth owning now:

- **Collinear** — points that lie on the *same line*. (A, B, C are collinear if one straight line passes through all three.)
- **Congruent** ($\cong$) — exactly the **same shape and size**. Two segments are congruent when they have the same length.
- **Equal** ($=$) — the **same value**. We say two *measurements* are equal ($AB = CD$, both 5 cm) and two *figures* are congruent ($\overline{AB} \cong \overline{CD}$). Same idea, careful wording.
- **Similar** — the **same shape but not necessarily the same size** (a photo and its enlargement).

## Watch it work

**A ray vs. its opposite.** Draw a straight line through points A, B, C in that order. $\overrightarrow{BA}$ and $\overrightarrow{BC}$ share the endpoint B but point opposite ways — together they rebuild the whole line. These are **opposite rays**.

**Naming a segment two ways.** $\overline{AB}$ and $\overline{BA}$ are the *same* segment — a segment doesn't care which end you name first (there's no "direction," just the piece between). But for rays, order is everything. Keep that difference straight and half of Lesson 1's tricky questions become easy.

**How many points on a line?** Infinitely many. Between any two points you name, there's always another. A line is *dense* with points — which is exactly why a single point can pin down an exact location: you're choosing one out of infinitely many.

## Why this matters

The next time you see a blueprint, a game map, or a 3D model, look closely: it is nothing but points, segments, and the occasional ray, arranged on purpose. This week you'll **build** those figures with your hands and in CAD, then **prove** you can tell them apart on sight. Master these four, and every shape in geometry is just a way of combining them.
