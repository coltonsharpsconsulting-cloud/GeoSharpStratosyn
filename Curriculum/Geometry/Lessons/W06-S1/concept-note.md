# Parallel & Perpendicular Lines; Bisectors

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a pencil, a straightedge, and a compass in hand, drawing each figure as it comes up. Last week you sorted every angle you could find into five types by their measure. This week two whole lines take the stage, and you meet the tool that cuts anything — an angle or a segment — exactly in half.*

## The one big question

Two straight lines share one flat plane forever. There are only **two** special ways they can ever relate to each other — what are they? And what does it take to cut something, an angle or a segment, into two perfectly equal halves?

## Two lines, two relationships

| Relationship | What it means | Symbol |
|---|---|---|
| **Parallel lines** | Same plane, same direction, forever — they never intersect no matter how far you extend them. | $\ell \parallel m$ |
| **Perpendicular lines** | Intersect, and form a **right angle** (90°) at the point where they cross. | $\ell \perp m$ |

- Parallel lines never drift closer or farther apart — think of the two rails of a train track, or the ruled lines on notebook paper.
- Perpendicular lines only need **one** right angle where they cross for the name to apply — geometry then guarantees the other three angles at that crossing are also 90° (you'll get to prove exactly why, once you meet vertical and supplementary angles next lesson).

## Bisectors — cutting exactly in half

- A **bisector** is a line, ray, or segment that cuts an **angle** or a **segment** into two congruent (equal) parts. Math-U-See's own wording: *"a line that cuts an angle or a line segment in half."*
- Two flavors: bisecting an **angle** splits one angle into two equal, smaller angles; bisecting a **segment** splits one segment into two equal, shorter segments.
- A **perpendicular bisector** is a special bisector of a segment — it does two jobs at once: it cuts the segment exactly in half **and** it crosses the segment at a right angle (90°).
- **Careful — not every bisector of a segment is a perpendicular bisector.** Infinitely many lines pass through a segment's midpoint, and every one of them bisects the segment. Only **one** of those lines also crosses at exactly 90° — that one line alone earns the name *perpendicular* bisector.

## Midpoint

- The **midpoint** is the one point that divides a segment into two congruent halves.
- MUS's definition, straight off the Lesson Practice page: if points A, B, D lie on a line in that order and $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{BD}$, then **B is the midpoint of $\overline{AD}$.**

## Straightedge & compass: finding these without a ruler

Two classic constructions — this week's real payoff. No ruler, no protractor: just circles (arcs) and a straight line, and you land exactly on the answer every time.

**Finding a segment's midpoint & perpendicular bisector**
1. Open your compass wider than half the segment's length, and don't change it.
2. Point the compass on one endpoint; swing one arc above the segment and one below.
3. Same width, point on the *other* endpoint; swing two more arcs, crossing the first pair.
4. Draw a straight line through the two arc-crossings. That line **is** the perpendicular bisector — and exactly where it crosses the original segment is the **midpoint**.

**Bisecting an angle**
1. Point the compass on the vertex; swing one arc that crosses **both** rays (sides) of the angle.
2. From each of those two crossing points, swing equal-width arcs into the interior of the angle so they cross each other.
3. Draw a ray from the vertex through that new crossing point. That ray **bisects** the angle — two exactly equal halves.

Session 3 is where you argue out loud *why* two arcs can never miss.

## Watch it work

**The "any line vs. the one line" trap.** Draw segment $\overline{CD}$. Now draw five different lines through its midpoint, fanning out like spokes on a wheel. Every single one of them **bisects** $\overline{CD}$ — cuts it into two congruent halves. But only the one running perpendicular to $\overline{CD}$ gets to be called *the* perpendicular bisector. Same word, "bisector," very different amount of information once you add "perpendicular."

**A straight-line check.** Draw parallel lines $\ell$ and $m$ as two horizontal rails. Now draw a third line perpendicular to $\ell$. Measure the angle that third line makes with $m$ too — still 90°, even though you never touched $m$ directly. (You'll argue exactly why next lesson, when a line crossing both parallels at once gets its own name: a *transversal*.)

## Why this matters

Every parking lot's painted lines, every picture frame's square corner, every wall framed to stand up straight — all of it depends on someone finding a true perpendicular, and often a true midpoint, with nothing fancier than a straightedge and a compass (or their modern equivalents). This week you'll **build** both classic constructions with your own hands, then **prove** why swinging two matched arcs can never miss the mark.
