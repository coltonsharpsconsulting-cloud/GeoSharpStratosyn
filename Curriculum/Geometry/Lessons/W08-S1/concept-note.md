# Transversals & Parallel Lines

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a pencil in hand, sketching two parallel lines and one slanted crossing line as it comes up. Lesson 6 taught you to pair up two angles at a single crossing point — adjacent, vertical, complementary, supplementary. This week the crossing point doubles: one line cuts across two others, and a whole new set of pairings appears between the two intersections.*

## The one big question

When a single line cuts across two others, eight new angles appear all at once. Most of the time those eight angles could be anything. But when the two lines being cut are **parallel**, several of those angles are locked together — which ones, and how far does that promise reach in reverse?

## The vocabulary of a crossing

| Term | What it is | How you spot it |
|---|---|---|
| **Transversal** | A straight line that crosses two (or more) other straight lines, each at its own point | The one "cutting" line — draw it and you get two brand-new intersections |
| **Interior angles** | The four angles that sit in the strip *between* the two lines being crossed | Angles closest to the middle — boxed in by both lines |
| **Exterior angles** | The four angles that sit *outside* the two lines being crossed | Above the top line, or below the bottom line |
| **Corresponding angles** | Two angles in the **same position** at each of the two crossings | Both "upper-left," both "upper-right," and so on |
| **Alternate interior angles** | Interior angles on **opposite sides** of the transversal | Interior, but one left of the transversal, one right |
| **Alternate exterior angles** | Exterior angles on **opposite sides** of the transversal | Exterior, but one left of the transversal, one right |

Read that table twice. Every one of these six words is describing *position* — where an angle sits relative to the two lines and the transversal — not its size. The size only gets guaranteed once you add one more fact:

> **The parallel promise.** If the two lines cut by the transversal are **parallel**, then corresponding angles are congruent, alternate interior angles are congruent, and alternate exterior angles are congruent. If the two lines are *not* parallel, none of that is guaranteed — the angles can be anything.

## The eight angles, numbered

Picture two parallel lines, both crossed by one slanted transversal. Label the top crossing point **M** and the bottom crossing point **R** — the same letters Math-U-See uses for exactly this figure. At each crossing, number the four angles going clockwise from upper-left:

- At **M** (top line): ∠1 upper-left, ∠2 upper-right, ∠3 lower-left, ∠4 lower-right.
- At **R** (bottom line): ∠5 upper-left, ∠6 upper-right, ∠7 lower-left, ∠8 lower-right.

Now the six words above turn into exact pairs:
- **Interior** = ∠3, ∠4, ∠5, ∠6 (the four angles boxed between the lines). **Exterior** = ∠1, ∠2, ∠7, ∠8.
- **Corresponding** pairs (same corner, different crossing): ∠1 & ∠5, ∠2 & ∠6, ∠3 & ∠7, ∠4 & ∠8.
- **Alternate interior** pairs (interior, opposite sides of the transversal): ∠3 & ∠6, ∠4 & ∠5.
- **Alternate exterior** pairs (exterior, opposite sides of the transversal): ∠1 & ∠8, ∠2 & ∠7.

If the two lines are parallel, all seven of those pairs are congruent pairs — but notice there are really only **two** different angle measures among all eight angles: one value and its supplement (180° minus that value).

## Watch it work

**Corresponding compute.** Two parallel lines are cut by a transversal. If m∠1 = 115°, then m∠5 = **115°** too — ∠1 and ∠5 are corresponding angles, and corresponding angles are congruent when the lines are parallel.

**Alternate exterior compute.** Same figure. ∠1 and ∠8 are alternate exterior angles, so m∠8 = **115°** as well — even though ∠8 sits at the *other* crossing, on the *opposite* side of the transversal from ∠1.

**The converse — running the promise backward.** Given corresponding angles are congruent, then the lines crossed by the transversal *must* be parallel. Given alternate interior angles are congruent, the lines *must* be parallel. This is the converse of the parallel promise above, and it's just as strong: congruent angles → parallel lines, with no protractor ever touching the lines themselves.

## A callback to angle pairs

Lesson 6 proved vertical angles are congruent because both are supplements of the *same* adjacent angle. That exact move proves alternate interior angles congruent here: at crossing M, ∠4 and ∠1 are **vertical angles** (Lesson 6), so m∠4 = m∠1. And ∠1 corresponds to ∠5 across the two parallel lines, so m∠1 = m∠5. Chain the two together — m∠4 = m∠1 = m∠5 — and you've proven ∠4 ≅ ∠5, an alternate interior pair, using nothing but Lesson 6's vertical angles and the parallel promise. You'll build that exact argument yourself in Session 3.

## Why this matters

A crosswalk cuts across two parallel curb lines. A ladder leans across two parallel fence rails. Ruled notebook paper crossed by a diagonal pencil stroke is a transversal cutting parallel lines, every time you doodle. Surveyors and framers use the *converse* constantly: measure one pair of corresponding angles with a transit or a speed square, and if they match, you've proven two boards — or two property lines — are truly parallel, without ever laying a straightedge between them. This week you'll **hunt** a real transversal and measure its angle pairs, then **prove**, in words, why the congruence can never fail when the lines are parallel.
