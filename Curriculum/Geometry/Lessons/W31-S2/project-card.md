# Indirect Measurement Lab

*A tree, a flagpole, a building — none of them care that your tape measure only reaches eight feet up. Today you measure one of them anyway, using nothing but a shadow (or a mirror), a stick of known height, and the same AA similarity that let you solve a proportion on paper yesterday.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a measurement log (known height, two shadow or sightline distances, your proportion, and your computed height) plus a cross-check, photographed for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A yardstick, meter stick, or any stick/pole of a height you can measure exactly
- A measuring tape (long enough for shadows — 25 ft / 8 m or more is ideal)
- A calculator
- A small flat mirror (for the mirror method)
- Sidewalk chalk or a small marker (to mark spots on the ground)
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — Choose Your Method & Your Object
1. Pick a tall object you cannot reach the top of directly: a tree, a flagpole, a basketball hoop, a building corner, a light pole.
2. Choose your method based on today's conditions: the **shadow method** needs direct sun and clear shadows; the **mirror method** works anytime there's flat, level ground between you and the object.

## Part 2 — Shadow Method
3. Stand your stick perfectly vertical on level ground. Measure its exact height.
4. At the SAME moment (shadows shift as the sun moves), measure the stick's shadow length and the tall object's shadow length, both from the same flat ground.
5. The stick-and-its-shadow forms one right triangle; the object-and-its-shadow forms a second. Both share the sun's angle AND a right angle where each one stands straight up — by AA, the two triangles are similar.
6. Set up the proportion: $\dfrac{\text{stick height}}{\text{stick shadow}} = \dfrac{\text{object height}}{\text{object shadow}}$, and cross-multiply to solve for the object's height.

## Part 3 — Mirror Method (alternative or cross-check)
7. Lay the mirror flat on level ground, somewhere between you and the object's base.
8. Back away slowly, watching the mirror, until you see the very top of the object centered in it.
9. Measure three lengths: your eye height (straight up from the ground where you're standing), your horizontal distance from the mirror, and the mirror's horizontal distance from the object's base.
10. The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection, so your eye-to-mirror triangle and the object-to-mirror triangle share that angle plus a right angle at the ground — similar by AA, same as the shadow method. Set up the matching proportion: $\dfrac{\text{eye height}}{\text{your distance to mirror}} = \dfrac{\text{object height}}{\text{mirror's distance to object}}$, and solve.
11. If time allows, do BOTH methods on the same object and compare your two computed heights.

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch both right triangles to scale using your real measurements, constrain the shared angle equal on both, and let the software confirm the proportional side lengths — then export a screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Work on flat, even ground and watch your footing while walking backward for the mirror method. Never look directly at the sun while sighting shadows — watch the shadow on the ground, not the sky.
