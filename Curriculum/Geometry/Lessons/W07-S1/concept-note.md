# Supplementary, Complementary & Vertical Angles

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a pencil in hand, sketching two crossing lines and marking each pair as it comes up. Lesson 4 sorted a single angle into a family by its own degree measure. Lesson 5 gave you the compass to bisect a segment or an angle exactly in half. This week two angles get compared to *each other* — and one of those comparisons turns out to be true no matter what anyone measures.*

## The one big question

Two angles can sit anywhere in the world, totally unrelated. But sometimes their measures are locked together by an exact rule — and one of those rules is a promise that never breaks. What are the four ways two angles get paired up, and which pairing is guaranteed?

## Four ways two angles get paired up

| Term | What ties the two angles together | How you spot it |
|---|---|---|
| **Adjacent angles** | Share the same vertex **and** a common side (ray) — and don't overlap | Two angle "wedges" sitting right next to each other, touching along one shared ray |
| **Vertical angles** | The two angles directly across the vertex from two crossing lines | Share **only** the vertex, never a side — and are **always congruent** |
| **Complementary angles** | Two angles whose measures **add up to 90°** | Together they'd exactly fill a right-angle corner |
| **Supplementary angles** | Two angles whose measures **add up to 180°** | Together they'd exactly fill a straight line |

Read that table twice, then notice what it does *not* say: complementary and supplementary angles don't have to touch, share a vertex, or sit anywhere near each other — the only requirement is what their two measures **add up to**. Adjacent and vertical, on the other hand, are entirely about *position* — where the angles sit relative to one point.

- **Adjacent** is a position word. ∠AHC and ∠CHB are adjacent if they share vertex H and ray HC, and neither one's interior overlaps the other's.
- **Vertical** angles are born from two intersecting lines. Two straight lines crossing at one point always make **four** angles, and they pair up into **two** sets of vertical angles — the ones directly opposite each other, like the top and bottom of an hourglass.
- **Complementary** and **supplementary** are sum words. m∠A + m∠B = 90° makes them complementary; m∠A + m∠B = 180° makes them supplementary. Nothing about position is required — a 30° angle drawn in the corner of this page and a 60° angle drawn in the next room are still complementary.

> **The linear pair — where adjacent meets supplementary.** When two *adjacent* angles happen to have outer sides that form one straight line (opposite rays), they are called a **linear pair** — and a linear pair is *always* supplementary. That's not a coincidence: the two angles together fill exactly one straight angle, 180°, because that's what a straight line **is**.

## Where vertical angles come from

Draw two straight lines crossing at point H. Going around H, you meet four angles in order: call them ∠AHC, ∠CHB, ∠BHD, ∠DHA. Each angle is **adjacent** to the two angles next to it (they form linear pairs, each summing to 180°) — but each angle is **vertical** to the one angle directly across the point from it: ∠AHC and ∠BHD are vertical; ∠CHB and ∠DHA are vertical.

## Watch it work

**Complementary compute.** ∠A and ∠B are complementary. If m∠A = 37°, then m∠B = 90° − 37° = **53°**. Whatever the first angle measures, the second is whatever is left to reach 90°.

**Supplementary compute.** ∠P and ∠Q are supplementary. If m∠P = 118°, then m∠Q = 180° − 118° = **62°** — whatever is left to reach a straight line.

**Vertical angles, without measuring them.** Two lines cross at H, making ∠1, ∠2, ∠3, ∠4 in order around the point. ∠1 and ∠2 are a linear pair, so m∠1 + m∠2 = 180°. ∠2 and ∠3 are *also* a linear pair, so m∠2 + m∠3 = 180°. Both equations say something equals "180° minus m∠2" — which means m∠1 and m∠3 **must be the same number**, even though nobody measured either one directly. That argument — two angles are congruent because they're both supplements of the *same* angle — is exactly what you'll be asked to defend in Session 3.

## A callback to the compass

Lesson 5 handed you a compass and straightedge to bisect a segment or an angle without ever touching a ruler or protractor. That skill folds right back in this week: this lesson's Systematic Review has you bisecting a segment and a 52° angle again, cold, with just the two tools. If a supplementary pair happens to be a linear pair split down the middle, bisecting it gives you two 90° angles — a right angle, built with nothing but a compass.

## Why this matters

A pair of scissors opening is two adjacent angles at the pivot. Fold a square napkin corner-to-corner and you've cut a right angle into two complementary 45° pieces. A crossroads seen from above is four angles at one point — two vertical pairs, guaranteed equal whether or not the road crews measured them that way. This week you'll **hunt** those real pairs and **build** a physical "X" to confirm vertical angles come out equal, then **prove**, in words, why that equality can never fail.
