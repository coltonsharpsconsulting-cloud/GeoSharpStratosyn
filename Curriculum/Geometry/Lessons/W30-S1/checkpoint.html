L26 — Right-Triangle Congruence: HL, LL, HA, LA

Week 30 · Right-Triangle Congruence — HL, LL, HA, LA

1.Two right triangles each have a hypotenuse and ONE leg marked congruent. Which shortcut proves them congruent?

2.The LL shortcut is really which general congruence postulate in disguise?

3.Right triangles ΔJKL and ΔMNO are both right-angled at K and N. JL ≅ MO (hypotenuses) and JK ≅ MN (one leg each). Explain, using the Pythagorean theorem, why the triangles must be congruent even though only two parts (plus the right angle) are marked congruent.

Answer
With the hypotenuse and one leg fixed, the Pythagorean theorem forces the third leg's length (the other leg = √(hyp² − leg²)) to come out the same for both triangles. All three sides now match, which is really SSS — that's why HL is valid even though Side-Side-Angle is not valid in general.

4.On the grid, draw two separate right triangles that satisfy the LA shortcut: mark the right angle in each, make one leg 4 units long in each, and mark a 40° acute angle adjacent to that leg (between the right angle and the 40° angle) in each. Label which general postulate (ASA or AAS) this arrangement matches.

Answer
Two right triangles, each with the right angle marked, a 4-unit leg, and a 40° angle adjacent to that leg (the leg between the right angle and the 40° angle) — labeled ASA, since the leg is the included side between the two given angles.

5.Two right triangles each have their hypotenuse marked congruent, and one ACUTE angle (not the right angle) marked congruent. Which shortcut applies?

6.Explain why HA is really just a renamed version of AAS, naming the two angles and the side involved.

Answer
The two angles are the right angle (always congruent, since both are 90°) and the given acute angle. The hypotenuse is the side opposite the right angle, which is NOT between the two named angles — so it's angle-angle-side, i.e., AAS.
