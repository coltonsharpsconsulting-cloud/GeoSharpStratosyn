# Set Search / Plane Hunt

*Find a real plane, then sort real objects into a real union and a real intersection. When you have to decide, by hand, whether the marble belongs in the overlap — union and intersection stop being symbols on a page and become a decision you can defend.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a photo of your two loops sorted, plus your named plane, for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- Two hula hoops, or two loops of string/yarn in different colors (one for Set A, one for Set B)
- 10–15 small household objects to sort (coins, spoons, marbles, rubber bands, keys, wooden blocks, etc.)
- A flat surface to serve as your "plane" — a tabletop, a wall, a section of floor
- Index cards or sticky notes + a marker, for labels
- A ruler or tape measure (for marking your three points)
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Set it up
1. **Choose your plane.** Pick a real flat surface — a tabletop, a wall, a section of floor. Say out loud why it counts as a plane: it's flat (no bumps or curves), it has length **and** width, and it keeps going past the edges you can see.
2. **Choose two categories likely to overlap.** For example, Set A = "things that are round," Set B = "things that are metal." Write each category on a card and set it beside a loop.
3. **Lay your two loops so they overlap** in the middle of your plane, like a Venn diagram you can walk into.

## Sort it
4. Gather 10+ small objects. For each one, decide: does it belong in **A only**, **B only**, **both** (the overlap — the intersection), or **neither** (leave it outside both loops)?
5. Physically place each object in its correct region. Anything that's neither round nor metal stays outside both loops — it isn't part of A ∪ B at all.
6. Once everything is sorted, write down what's in **A ∪ B** (everything inside either loop) and what's in **A ∩ B** (only the shared middle).

## Name the plane
7. On your plane's surface, mark **three points that are not all on one line** (use sticky dots or marker taps — spread them out, don't put them in a row). Label them and write the plane's name using all three, e.g. "plane RST."

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, model a **plane** as a Sketch, then add two **construction lines** that cross inside it. Show that their intersection is a single point. If you want to go further, add a second sketch plane and note (in words) that two planes would meet along a line, not a point — we'll build that in CAD properly in a later unit. Export a screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Keep loops and string flat on the floor to avoid tripping; use only objects that are safe to handle (nothing sharp, hot, or breakable); keep small parts away from younger siblings or pets.

## Turn in (rubric — 8 pts)
| Criterion | Pts |
|---|---|
| Plane identified and explained (flat, has length + width, extends past its visible edges) | 1 |
| Two loops set up as overlapping Set A / Set B with labeled categories | 1 |
| At least 8 objects correctly sorted into A only / B only / both / neither | 2 |
| Union (A ∪ B) and intersection (A ∩ B) correctly written down | 2 |
| Three non-collinear points marked and the plane named using them | 1 |
| Photo taken of the finished, sorted layout | 1 |

Photograph the finished loops-and-objects layout and drop it in the [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]]. In Session 3 you'll defend, in words, why two planes can only ever meet along a line.
