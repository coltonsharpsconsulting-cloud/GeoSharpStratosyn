# Area of Parallelograms, Triangles & Trapezoids

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a pencil, a ruler, and a piece of grid paper in hand, sketching each shape as it comes up. Every angle pair last week measured a turn. This week two straight sides and the space **between** them get measured too — and that measurement is called area.*

## The one big question

A rectangle's area is easy: multiply its two sides. But a parallelogram leans, a triangle comes to a point, and a trapezoid has two different-length parallel sides. What one idea, borrowed from the rectangle, still rescues all three?

## Base and height — the idea that carries everything

Every area formula in this lesson starts from the same two measurements:

| Term | What it is | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|
| **Base** | Any one side you choose to measure along. | You can pick more than one side to call "the base" — the height just has to match whichever one you pick. |
| **Height** | The straight, **perpendicular** (⊥) distance from the base to the opposite side or vertex. | It is almost never the slanted side. A parallelogram's slanted side is longer than its height — measuring it and calling it the height is the single most common mistake in this lesson. |

> **The one rule that never breaks:** the height is always perpendicular to the base. If a segment doesn't cross the base at a right angle, it isn't the height — no matter how tempting the slanted side looks.

## Three shapes, three formulas

- **Parallelogram:** $A = b \cdot h$ — base times height, exactly like a rectangle. Slide a triangular sliver off one slanted end of a parallelogram and it snaps onto the other end, rebuilding a rectangle with the *same* base and the *same* height. The outline changed; the area never did.
- **Triangle:** $A = \tfrac{1}{2} b \cdot h$ — always **exactly half** of a rectangle sharing the same base and height. Take two congruent copies of any triangle, flip one, and fit it against the other along the slanted side: together they tile a parallelogram (or rectangle) with that base and height. One triangle is half of it, full stop — never a third, never two-thirds.
- **Trapezoid:** $A = \dfrac{b_1 + b_2}{2} \cdot h$ — the **average** of the two parallel bases, times the height. Math-U-See says it plainly: *"In order to find the area of a trapezoid, it is first necessary to find the average base."* Average the two bases first — then multiply.

## Watch it work

**Parallelogram.** Base 12 ft, height 5 ft → $A = 12 \times 5 = 60\text{ ft}^2$. The slanted side might measure 11.7 ft or 14 ft — it doesn't matter. Only the perpendicular height counts.

**Triangle.** Base 10 in, height 4 in → picture the full rectangle first: $10 \times 4 = 40\text{ in}^2$. The triangle is exactly half of it: $A = \tfrac12(10)(4) = 20\text{ in}^2$.

**Trapezoid.** Bases 6 units and 10 units, height 5 units → average the bases first: $(6+10) \div 2 = 8$. Then $A = 8 \times 5 = 40$ square units.

## Composite figures — add the parts

A figure that isn't one clean shape — an L-shaped room, a pennant with a rectangular tail, a tabletop with one trapezoid end — is just simpler shapes glued together. Split it with a dashed line into rectangles, triangles, and trapezoids you already know how to handle, find each piece's area, and **add them up**. Nothing new to learn — just the same three formulas, used more than once on one figure.

## Why this matters

A pennant, a tabletop, a roof, a garden bed, a road sign — almost nothing in the built world is a perfect rectangle. This week you'll **measure** real bases and heights and compute real areas, and **prove**, in your own words, why a triangle is always exactly half of a same-base-same-height rectangle, and why a trapezoid needs the average of its two bases rather than either one alone. Master base-and-height now, and every solid-shape and surface-area formula later this year builds on exactly this idea.
