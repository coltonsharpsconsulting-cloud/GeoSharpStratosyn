# Postulates, Theorems & Angle Relationships

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a pencil in hand, redrawing the two-crossing-lines figure from Lesson 6 and the parallel-lines-and-transversal figure from Lesson 7 as they come up. You have used "vertical angles are congruent" and "corresponding angles are congruent" all year. This week asks the question you've never had to answer before: **how do we actually know that's true?**

## The one big question

Some geometry facts are simply agreed to. Others are *proven* — built, step by step, out of the facts that came before them. If you had to sort everything you know about angles into "we just accept this" and "we can actually prove this," where would the line fall — and does it matter?

It matters enormously. Next week you start writing formal two-column proofs, and every single line of every proof you write will need a *reason*. This week is where those reasons get their names.

## Two kinds of true statements — and a third

| Term | What it is | How you know it's true |
|---|---|---|
| **Postulate** (also called an **axiom**) | A statement accepted as true *without* proof — one of the starting rules of the system | You don't prove it — you agree to it, because the whole system has to start somewhere |
| **Theorem** | A statement proven true *using* postulates, definitions, and earlier theorems | You (or someone) built a logical chain from accepted rules to this new fact |
| **Corollary** | A statement that follows almost immediately from a theorem, with little or no extra proof needed | It's a theorem's "free bonus" — a short hop from something already proven |

Math-U-See puts it plainly: *"An axiom or postulate is an observation that one assumes to be true. Statements that can be proven true are called theorems."* Read that twice. A postulate isn't weaker than a theorem — it's simply *earlier*. Every theorem, traced back far enough, rests on postulates. Geometry has to start somewhere that isn't itself proven, or nothing could ever get proven at all.

## Naming the angle relationships you already know

Here is the payoff: the angle facts you have used since Lesson 6 and Lesson 7 were never just "rules." Each one is either a postulate or a theorem, and each has a name.

| Rule | Kind | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Linear Pair Postulate** — if two angles form a linear pair, their measures sum to 180° | Postulate | Accepted outright — it's simply what a straight angle *is* |
| **Vertical Angles Theorem** — vertical angles are congruent | Theorem | **Proven** from the Linear Pair Postulate (see "Watch it work" below) |
| **Corresponding Angles Postulate** — if two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, corresponding angles are congruent | Postulate | Accepted as the defining behavior of parallel lines cut by a transversal |
| **Alternate Interior Angles Theorem** — …then alternate interior angles are congruent | Theorem | **Proven** by chaining the Vertical Angles Theorem to the Corresponding Angles Postulate |
| **Alternate Exterior Angles Theorem** — …then alternate exterior angles are congruent | Theorem | **Proven** the same way, one crossing over |

Notice the pattern: only **two** rules on this list are postulates — the Linear Pair Postulate and the Corresponding Angles Postulate. Everything else about angle pairs that you've ever used is a *theorem*, built on those two.

By contrast, "complementary" and "supplementary" aren't postulates *or* theorems at all — they're **definitions**. Two angles are supplementary because we *defined* the word that way, the same way "bisector" is defined as whatever divides an angle or a segment into two congruent parts (MUS's own fill-in-the-blank for this lesson).

## Watch it work

**Proving the Vertical Angles Theorem — the actual argument.** Two lines cross at H, making ∠1, ∠2, ∠3, ∠4 in order around H. ∠1 and ∠2 are a linear pair, so by the **Linear Pair Postulate**, m∠1 + m∠2 = 180° — which means m∠1 = 180° − m∠2. ∠2 and ∠3 are *also* a linear pair, so m∠3 = 180° − m∠2 as well. Both expressions equal exactly the same thing, "180° minus m∠2," so **m∠1 = m∠3**. That's it — that's the whole proof. Nobody measured anything; the Linear Pair Postulate alone forced the conclusion. This is precisely why vertical angles being congruent is a *theorem* and not its own separate postulate: it didn't need to be assumed, because it could be built.

**A corollary, live.** If one angle in a linear pair happens to be a right angle (90°), the Linear Pair Postulate forces its partner to be 90° too (180° − 90° = 90°). Chain that with the Vertical Angles Theorem and *all four* angles at the crossing turn out to be right angles. That quick, almost-free conclusion — "if two intersecting lines form one right angle, they form four" — is a **corollary** of the Linear Pair Postulate and the Vertical Angles Theorem together.

## Converses — running a statement backward

The **converse** of a statement swaps its hypothesis and its conclusion. The converse of "if two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, corresponding angles are congruent" is "if corresponding angles are congruent, then the two lines cut by the transversal are parallel." A converse is a *brand-new claim* — proving the original statement proves nothing about whether its converse is true. Sometimes a converse fails completely. But for the specific list of statements in this lesson, Math-U-See flags something unusual: *"All of the statements listed in this lesson also have converses that are true."* That's exactly how surveyors and framers work in reverse — measure one pair of corresponding angles, find them congruent, and you've just *proven* two boards are parallel without ever laying a straightedge between them.

## The tools that will run every future proof

Three more facts round out this lesson, and you'll reach for them by name in *every* two-column proof from here on:
- **Reflexive Property** — anything is congruent (or equal) to itself: ∠A ≅ ∠A.
- **Symmetric Property** — order doesn't matter: if ∠A ≅ ∠B, then ∠B ≅ ∠A.
- **Transitive Property** — chains carry through: if ∠A ≅ ∠B and ∠B ≅ ∠C, then ∠A ≅ ∠C.

Look back at the Vertical Angles Theorem proof above — the move where "both expressions equal 180° − m∠2, so they equal each other" *is* the Transitive Property in action. You have already been using these properties. This week just gives them their names.

## Why this matters

A courtroom argument, a scientific proof, and a geometry proof all work the same way: start from statements everyone already agrees to (postulates, evidence, axioms), and build forward one justified step at a time until the conclusion is forced, not guessed. You cannot skip a step and call it proven — that's the entire discipline. This week you sort your existing angle knowledge into what's assumed and what's earned, **build** a reference wall of every named rule with its own diagram, then **prove**, out loud, why the Vertical Angles Theorem had to be a theorem and could never have been its own postulate. Next unit, you pick up a two-column proof and write one yourself, using exactly these names in the "reasons" column.
