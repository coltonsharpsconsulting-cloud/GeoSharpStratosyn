# Volume of Pyramids, Cones, Prisms & Spheres

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a pencil in hand, sketching each solid as it comes up. Last week every solid stood up straight — a box, a cube, a cylinder — so volume was simply the area of the base times the height. This week three of your four solids don't stand up straight at all: two of them taper to a single point, and one of them has no flat base whatsoever.*

## The one big question

A rectangular solid and a cylinder both hold *exactly* base-area-times-height, no more and no less, because their sides run straight up from base to top. A pyramid and a cone don't do that — they narrow the whole way up until they vanish into a single point. If you built a pyramid and a box with the *exact same* base and the *exact same* height, would the pyramid hold the same volume as the box, more, or less? And by how much?

## Anatomy of a solid that comes to a point

| Term | What it is | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|
| **Apex** | The single point where a pyramid's triangular faces (or a cone's curved surface) all meet. | A rectangular solid has no apex — only a pyramid and a cone taper to one. |
| **Altitude** | The perpendicular height — a straight line from the apex down to the base, meeting it at a right angle. | This is the height that belongs in the volume formula. |
| **Slant height** | The height of *one triangular face* of a pyramid (or the straight distance from a cone's apex to its base along the surface) — measured along the tilted face, not straight down. | Always longer than the altitude. Using it in the volume formula by mistake is the single most common error in this lesson. |

Picture a tent: the pole running straight up its center is the **altitude**; the tent fabric sloping down from the peak to a corner is the **slant height**. Both are real measurements — only one of them belongs in the volume formula.

## Prism: the general shape a rectangular solid was a special case of

A **prism** is any solid with **two congruent, parallel bases** and lateral (side) surfaces that are always **parallelograms** — a rectangular solid is just the special case where that base happens to be a rectangle. The base of a prism can just as easily be a triangle, a pentagon, or any other polygon. The rule from last week still applies exactly as written, with no exceptions and no ⅓:

$$V = B \cdot h$$

where $B$ is the area of one base (found however you'd normally find that shape's area) and $h$ is the perpendicular distance between the two bases.

## The new idea: multiply by one third

Math-U-See's rule is direct: to find the volume of a pyramid or a cone, find the area of the base, multiply by the height — then multiply that answer by **one third**.

$$\text{Pyramid: } V = \tfrac{1}{3} B h \qquad\qquad \text{Cone: } V = \tfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$$

A pyramid's volume is exactly ⅓ the volume of a rectangular solid sharing its base and height; a cone's volume is exactly ⅓ the volume of a cylinder sharing its base and height — never ½, never ¼, always exactly a third. You'll prove that relationship with your own hands in the Build session, by pouring a pyramid-shaped container of rice or sand into a prism with the identical base and height, three times, and watching it come out even.

> **The one rule that never breaks:** find the area of the base first, multiply by the height — exactly like last week — and then, *only* if the solid comes to a point, multiply once more by ⅓. Nothing about the arithmetic is new; one factor just joins the party.

## Sphere: a formula with no base and no exception

A **sphere** has no flat base to measure at all — no polygon, no circle sitting underneath it — so it can't be built from "area of the base × height." Its formula stands completely on its own, using only the radius, cubed:

$$V = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3$$

Math-U-See asks you to round sphere-volume answers to the nearest hundredth, since $\pi$ never divides out evenly here the way it sometimes does with a multiple of 7.

## Watch it work

**Prism (triangular base).** A tent-shaped prism has a right-triangle base with legs 6 in and 4 in, and a length of 9 in: base area $= \tfrac{1}{2}(6)(4) = 12\text{ in}^2$, so $V = 12 \times 9 = 108\text{ in}^3$. No ⅓ — the sides run straight back, not to a point.

**Pyramid.** A square-based pyramid has a base edge of 6 cm and an altitude of 8 cm: base area $= 6^2 = 36\text{ cm}^2$, so $V = \tfrac{1}{3}(36)(8) = 96\text{ cm}^3$.

**Cone.** A cone has a radius of 3 ft and an altitude of 7 ft: base area $= \pi(3)^2 = 9\pi\text{ ft}^2$, so $V = \tfrac{1}{3}(9\pi)(7) = 21\pi \approx 65.97\text{ ft}^3$.

**Sphere.** A sphere has a radius of 5 in: $V = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi(5)^3 = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi(125) = \tfrac{500}{3}\pi \approx 523.60\text{ in}^3$ (rounded to the nearest hundredth).

## Why this matters

An ice-cream cone, a party hat, the Great Pyramid, a basketball — every one of these is a pyramid, a cone, a prism, or a sphere, and every one of them holds an exact number of cubic units, whether or not anyone bothers to compute it. This week you'll **measure and compute** the volume of real cones, pyramids, and spheres — or 3D-print/CAD-model one and verify it by water displacement — then **prove**, in your own words, why a pyramid or cone always holds exactly ⅓ of the prism or cylinder that shares its base and height. Master this one new factor, and every solid Math-U-See will ever ask you about is already in your hands.
