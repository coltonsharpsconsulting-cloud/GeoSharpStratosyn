# 3-4-5 String Lab

*Two thousand years of builders have squared a corner with nothing but a piece of string marked at 3, 4, and 5 units. Today you find out why it works — then go test the same rule against a real right angle in your own home.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a photographed 3-4-5 string triangle with its angle checked, plus a measured-and-computed real right angle, for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A length of string, twine, or rope (at least 4 ft / 120 cm) that won't stretch much
- A tape measure or ruler, marked in a consistent unit (inches, feet, or cm)
- Tape or a marker to mark knot positions; something to tie 3 knots/loops
- A protractor or a carpenter's/speed square
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — Build the 3-4-5 Triangle
1. Choose a unit (say, 1 ft or 30 cm). Measure and mark your string at 3 units, then 4 more units (7 units total), then 5 more units (12 units total), and tie the two ends together so the string forms a taut loop of 12 units around.
2. With a partner (or three fixed points — a nail, a chair leg, a piece of tape on the floor), pull the loop taut so the three marked segments (3, 4, and 5 units) each form a straight side of a triangle.
3. Use your protractor or square on the corner where the **3-unit** and **4-unit** sides meet. What is that angle?
4. Record your result. Why should this ALWAYS come out 90°, no matter how big or small your unit is? (Hint: check $3^2 + 4^2$ against $5^2$.)

## Part 2 — Test a Real Right Angle
5. Find one real right angle around your home — a ramp, a door-frame corner brace, a ladder leaned against a wall, a corner shelf bracket, the corner of a table.
6. Measure its two legs (the two sides that meet at the right angle) and its hypotenuse (the side across from the right angle) with your tape measure, in the same unit.
7. Compute $a^2 + b^2$ and $c^2$ for your three measurements. How close do they come?
8. Any gap is almost always the ruler, not the rule — real-world measuring is never perfectly exact, but the Pythagorean theorem is.

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch a 3-4-5 triangle with length constraints (3, 4, and 5 units on each side) and let the software's angle tool confirm the 90° corner exactly — then try a non-right trio like 3, 4, 6 and watch the corner refuse to land on 90°. Export a screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Keep string taut but don't overtighten around fingers; if using a ladder or ramp for Part 2, don't climb it — measure it where it safely rests.
