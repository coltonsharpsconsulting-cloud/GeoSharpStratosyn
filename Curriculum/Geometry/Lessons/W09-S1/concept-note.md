# Polygons, Quadrilaterals & Perimeter

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a pencil in hand, sketching each shape as it comes up. Last week you learned what a transversal does to a pair of parallel lines. This week you leave single angles behind and start closing shapes — and you pick up the tool for measuring the distance all the way around one.*

## The one big question

If you connect straight sides end to end until they close up into one figure, what do you have — and how many different, precisely-named shapes can four sides make?

## Polygons — where closed shapes start

A **polygon** is any closed, flat figure made entirely of straight sides. Polygons are named by how many sides they have: three sides make a **triangle**, four sides make a **quadrilateral** — any four-sided polygon at all, no matter how lopsided. Everything below is a *kind* of quadrilateral; the differences come down to two questions: **are any sides parallel, and are any sides or angles equal?**

## The quadrilateral family tree

| Shape | Defining property | 
|---|---|
| **Quadrilateral** | Any four-sided polygon. The whole family. |
| **Parallelogram** | Two pairs of parallel sides. Angles may or may not be right angles. |
| **Rectangle** | Four right angles **and** two pairs of parallel sides. |
| **Rhombus** | Four congruent sides. Angles may or may not be right angles. |
| **Square** | Four congruent sides **and** four right angles. |
| **Trapezoid** | Only **one** pair of parallel sides — the one branch that is *not* a parallelogram. |

Read the table as a chain of extra requirements, not six unrelated shapes:
- Every **rectangle** and every **rhombus** is a **parallelogram** — each one just adds one extra rule on top (right angles, or congruent sides).
- A **square** is a rectangle *with* congruent sides. A square is also a rhombus *with* right angles. Either description is correct, and both point at the same shape.
- A **trapezoid** breaks off on its own: it is only allowed *one* pair of parallel sides, so it can never also be a parallelogram, rectangle, rhombus, or square.

**This is the ∩ from Lesson 2, made visible.** Back then you let A = "quadrilaterals with four right angles" and B = "quadrilaterals with four equal sides," and found that a square sits in A ∩ B — the overlap. That wasn't just a numbers exercise: A is the rectangle family, B is the rhombus family, and their intersection is exactly the square. Squares ⊂ rectangles, squares ⊂ rhombi, and both rectangles and rhombi ⊂ parallelograms — the same subset arrows from Lesson 2, now drawn between shapes instead of number sets.

## Perimeter — the distance around

**Perimeter** is simply the distance around a figure — add up every side. For a rectangle or square that's just $P = 2(l + w)$ or $P = 4s$, but the definition never changes: *every side, added together.*

**Composite (L-shaped) figures.** Real floor plans and lots and rooms are rarely one clean rectangle — they step in and out. When a figure has a "notch" cut into one corner, first find any **missing side lengths** (the piece taken out and the piece left over always have to add back up to the full outer measurement), then **add every side of the actual boundary**, including the ones that step in and out. Skip a step and you'll undercount; the shortcut is there is no shortcut — every side counts once.

## Interior angle sums

The three angles inside **any** triangle always add up to exactly **180°** — no matter how stretched or skinny. The four angles inside **any** quadrilateral always add up to exactly **360°** — and here's why: draw **one diagonal** across any quadrilateral, corner to corner, and it splits cleanly into **two triangles**. Those two triangles share the diagonal but together cover every one of the quadrilateral's four original angles exactly once. Two triangles, 180° each: $180° + 180° = 360°$. Every time, for every quadrilateral, convex or not.

## Watch it work

**The missing-side trick.** A storage shed's footprint is a rectangle with one rectangular corner notch cut out — an L-shape, every angle a right angle. The overall length is 11 ft and the overall width is 7 ft; the notch removed from that corner is 4 ft by 3 ft. The two sides you're *not* given are found by subtraction: $11 - 4 = 7$ ft and $7 - 3 = 4$ ft. Now add all six sides of the actual boundary: $11 + 4 + 4 + 3 + 7 + 7 = 36$ ft. Notice the answer is exactly the same as the perimeter of the *original* rectangle before the notch was cut ($2(11+7) = 36$) — cutting a corner notch removes length from two sides but adds it right back as two new sides, so the total never changes.

**The 360° check.** Sketch any four-sided figure, even a weird kite shape, and measure its four angles with a protractor. Add them. Every single time, no matter how the figure is drawn, the total comes out to 360° — because it's always secretly two 180° triangles glued along one diagonal.

## Why this matters

Every floor plan, city lot, picture frame, and road sign is some member of this quadrilateral family, and every fence, baseboard, and property line you'll ever measure is a perimeter. This week you'll **classify and measure** real quadrilaterals and **build** your own set of the family with your hands, then **prove** why the hierarchy only runs one direction — every square is a rectangle, but not every rectangle is a square — and why 360° never changes. Master this family tree now, and next week's lesson (area) reuses every one of these same shapes.
