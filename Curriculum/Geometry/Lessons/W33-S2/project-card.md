# Sight & Measure — A Real Clinometer, A Real Height

*Sine, cosine, and tangent stop being algebra the moment you point a homemade tool at something too tall to climb. Today you build a clinometer, sight a real angle of elevation, and use tangent to calculate a height you never touch — for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a photographed clinometer sighting a real tall object, with the angle, distance, eye height, and calculated height all recorded, for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A protractor (any size, degrees marked 0-180)
- A drinking straw
- Tape
- A piece of string, about 8 inches, with a small weight tied to one end (a washer, a nut, or a paperclip works)
- A long tape measure, a trundle wheel, or a known stride length for pacing off distance
- A calculator (with sin/cos/tan keys, set to degree mode)
- A phone inclinometer/level app, if you have one (a valid substitute for the homemade clinometer)
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — Build the Clinometer
1. Tape the drinking straw flush along the flat (0°-180°) edge of the protractor — this is what you'll sight through.
2. Tie the weighted string through the small hole at the protractor's center (its origin), so the string hangs straight down and swings freely — this is your plumb line.
3. Hold the protractor with the straw's edge level and the flat side up; when you tilt the straw to sight something, the string will swing across the degree scale and mark your angle.

## Part 2 — Sight & Measure
4. Choose a real tall object — a tree, a flagpole, a basketball hoop, the peak of a roof — and a flat, open spot to stand.
5. Measure and record a fixed horizontal distance from the base of the object to where you're standing (20-50 ft usually gives a good sight line).
6. Sight the very top of the object through the straw. Have a partner read where the weighted string crosses the protractor's scale, or read it yourself once it settles.
7. Convert that reading to the angle of elevation: if you read the angle from the straw's edge to the string, **90° minus that reading** is your angle above horizontal (a phone inclinometer app usually gives you the angle of elevation directly — no subtraction needed).
8. Measure your own eye height off the ground with the tape measure — the clinometer sights from eye level, not the ground, so this gets added back at the end.

## Part 3 — Compute the Height
9. Set up tan(angle of elevation) = (height above eye level) / (horizontal distance), and solve for the unknown height above eye level.
10. Add your eye height back in to get the object's total height from the ground.
11. Sanity-check: does the number feel right compared to how tall the object looks standing next to you?

## Alternative / extension
No protractor on hand? A phone's built-in level or a free inclinometer app gives the angle of elevation directly — skip straight to Part 2, step 5. For an extension, sight and calculate a second real object's height, or sight the same object from a second distance and check that both trials predict the same height within a foot or two.

## Safety
Stand well clear of traffic, ledges, and tripping hazards while looking upward through the straw — pick your footing before you tilt your head back. Never sight anywhere near the sun. If your object is near a street or driveway, have an adult with you.
