# Quadrilateral Hunt & Build

*Find real quadrilaterals in your world and measure their perimeter, then build your own set of the family with your hands. When you push a hinged rectangle's corners and watch it lean into a parallelogram — same four sides, same perimeter, totally different angles — the family tree stops being a table and becomes something you felt happen.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a hunt log (two measured, classified real quadrilaterals) plus a photo of your built family set, for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A ruler or tape measure
- Cardstock strips or drinking straws (at least 12–16, cut to a few different lengths), or a scrap of foam board and pins/string as an alternative
- Brass fasteners (brads), or loose loops of tape, to hinge the corners of at least one shape so it can pivot
- Scissors, a marker, and a protractor or any square (rigid right-angle) corner — an index card corner works — for checking right angles
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — Quadrilateral Hunt (measure real shapes)
1. Find **two** different real quadrilaterals in your world — pick shapes that AREN'T both plain rectangles, for example: a rectangular door **and** a leaning gate or trellis panel, a square tile **and** a non-square picture frame, a tabletop **and** a kite or diamond-shaped sign.
2. For each one: measure and record all **four side lengths**, check the corners with your square/protractor to see which angles (if any) are right angles, **classify** it using the family tree (square? rectangle? parallelogram? rhombus? trapezoid? or just a general quadrilateral?), and **add the four sides** to find its perimeter.

## Part 2 — Build the Family (build it with your hands)
3. Cut straws or cardstock strips to specific lengths (plan them out first — e.g., four 12 cm strips for a rhombus/square, two 15 cm + two 10 cm for a rectangle).
4. Assemble **at least three** different quadrilateral family members — for example a square, a rectangle, and a non-rectangle parallelogram (or a rhombus).
5. On **at least one** shape, use brads or a loose loop of tape at the corners so the whole thing can pivot. Push two opposite corners toward each other and watch what happens: the side lengths don't change, but the angles — and the shape's name — do.
6. Verify every shape you build: measure its sides with the ruler and check its corners with the square/protractor before you call it done. Label each one with its family name and compute its perimeter (sum of its four side lengths).

## Safety
Scissors and brad points are sharp — cut and push away from your hand. If a tape measure snaps back, keep your fingers clear of the case. Keep small parts (brads, pins) away from younger siblings or pets.

## Turn in (rubric — 8 pts)
| Criterion | Pts |
|---|---|
| Two real quadrilaterals measured (all four sides) and correctly classified | 2 |
| Both real quadrilaterals' perimeters correctly computed | 1 |
| At least three built quadrilateral family members, correctly assembled | 2 |
| At least one built shape hinged/adjustable, with angles and sides checked | 1 |
