# Volume of Rectangular Solids & Cylinders

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a pencil in hand, sketching each solid as it comes up. Circumference and area last week were both flat, two-dimensional measurements. This week a third dimension — height — stacks on top of a base, and a brand-new kind of number, volume, tells you how much space the whole solid fills.*

## The one big question

Area told you, in one number, how much surface a flat shape covers. A rectangular box or a soup can isn't flat — it has empty space *inside* it. What one idea, borrowed straight from area, tells you exactly how many little cubes it would take to fill a solid completely?

## Anatomy of a solid — face, edge, vertex

| Part | What it is | On a cardboard box |
|---|---|---|
| **Face** | One flat surface of the solid. | Each of the six cardboard panels. |
| **Edge** | The line segment where two faces meet. | Every crease/fold line on the box. |
| **Vertex** | The point where edges meet. | Each corner of the box. |

A **rectangular solid** has **6 faces**, **12 edges**, and **8 vertices**. A **cube** is simply a rectangular solid whose six faces are all **congruent squares** — same count of faces, edges, and vertices, just a stricter shape.

A **cylinder** is different: its base is a **circle**, not a polygon, and its curved side runs **perpendicular** to that circular base — the same way a rectangular solid's upright sides run perpendicular to its rectangular base.

## The one idea that carries everything: volume = area of the base × height

Math-U-See states the master rule plainly: **the basic formula for volume is the area of the base, times the height.** Every volume formula in this lesson is just that one sentence, applied to a different base:

- **Rectangular solid:** the base is a rectangle, area $= l \cdot w$, so $V = l \cdot w \cdot h$ — length times width times height.
- **Cube:** a rectangular solid where $l = w = h$, so $V = s^3$ (edge length cubed).
- **Cylinder:** the base is a circle, area $= \pi r^2$, so $V = \pi r^2 \cdot h$ — the same base-times-height idea, just with a circle's area standing in for a rectangle's.

> **The one rule that never breaks:** find the area of the *base* first — a flat, 2-D calculation you already know — then multiply by the height. Nothing about volume is new arithmetic; it's area with one more multiplication.

## Cubic units — a whole new kind of unit

Length is measured in **linear units** (in, ft, cm). Area is measured in **square units** (in², ft², cm²) — a flat tile, two dimensions. Volume is measured in **cubic units** (in³, ft³, cm³) — a little cube, three dimensions. Picture packing a box with 1-inch sugar cubes, no gaps, no overlaps: however many cubes it takes to fill the box exactly, that's its volume in cubic inches. That mental picture — stacking identical unit cubes, or identical *layers* the size of the base, one on top of the next up to the full height — is exactly why area of the base times height works.

## Watch it work

**Rectangular solid.** Length 8 cm, width 5 cm, height 6 cm → base area $= 8 \times 5 = 40\text{ cm}^2$, then $V = 40 \times 6 = 240\text{ cm}^3$.

**Cube.** Edge 3 in → $V = 3 \times 3 \times 3 = 27\text{ in}^3$.

**Cylinder.** Radius 4 ft, height 10 ft → base area $= \pi (4)^2 = 16\pi\text{ ft}^2$, then $V = 16\pi \times 10 = 160\pi \approx 502.7\text{ ft}^3$. Notice the radius gets **squared** before anything else happens — using the diameter by mistake, or forgetting to square the radius, is the single most common volume mistake with cylinders.

## Why this matters

A cereal box, a soup can, a moving box, a water tank, the room you're sitting in right now — every one of them is a rectangular solid or a cylinder (or built from a few of them), and every one of them holds an exact number of cubic units whether or not anyone ever measures it. This week you'll **measure and compute** the volume of real containers — or 3D-print and CAD-model a solid and verify it by water displacement or by packing it with unit cubes — then **prove**, in your own words, why stacking identical layers of a base always gives you the exact volume. Master base-times-height now, and every solid in Lesson 15 — pyramids, cones, prisms, spheres — is just one more variation on this same idea.
