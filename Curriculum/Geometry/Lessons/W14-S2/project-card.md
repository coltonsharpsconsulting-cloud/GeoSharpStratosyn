# Circle Hunt & Build

*Find three real circles hiding in your everyday world and measure their radius and diameter like a surveyor would. Then flip the skill around: draw your own circle with a compass and label every part it has — center, radius, diameter, chord, arc, sector, and tangent.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a hunt log (three measured real-world circles, plus one sector example and one chord example) and a compass-drawn circle with every part labeled, photographed for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A compass (for drawing the circle) and a ruler or tape measure
- A protractor (to check the tangent's right angle) and a sharp pencil
- Blank or grid paper
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo
- Three real circular objects to measure — pick from: a plate, jar lid, or coin; a clock face; a bicycle or car wheel (parked and still); a round table
- One real example of a **sector** (a cut pizza or pie slice, a fan's blade sweep, a pie chart) and one real example of a **chord that is not a diameter** (a taut cable or string strung between two points on a circular fountain, arch, or trampoline frame; a chord line on a round window)

## Part 1 — Circle Hunt (measure real circles)
1. Find three different circular objects. For each one:
   - Lay your ruler across the middle, through the widest point, to measure the **diameter**.
   - Divide that measurement by 2 to get the **radius** — or measure straight from the center to the edge directly, if you can find the center.
2. Record what the object was and both measurements (to the nearest 0.5 cm or nearest 1/4 inch), and check that your two numbers actually satisfy $d = 2r$.
3. Point out (a photo or a sentence is fine) one real **sector** and one real **chord that is not a diameter** from the list above, and explain in one sentence why each fits that part's definition.

## Part 2 — Compass Build (draw & label every part)
4. Set your compass to a fixed radius (2–3 inches is plenty) and draw one circle on paper.
5. Mark the **center** with a small dot and label it C. Then draw and label, all on this one circle:
   - A **radius** (center to the rim)
   - A **diameter** (a chord straight through C) — write its length and confirm it's double the radius
   - A **chord** that is **not** the diameter
   - An **arc** — mark two points on the rim and trace over the shorter arc between them in a different color
   - A **sector** — shade the pie-slice region between two radii and the arc that connects them
   - A **tangent** line — using a straightedge, draw a line that just grazes the circle at one point only
6. **Check your tangent.** Lay your protractor where the tangent touches the circle and confirm the angle between the tangent and the radius drawn to that same point reads as close to 90° as you can manage.
7. If there's room, add a **secant** — a line through the same two points you used for your chord, extended past the circle on both ends — and note that it crosses the circle at exactly two points, unlike the tangent's one.

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch a circle with a radius constraint (type the exact value instead of eyeballing it), then add a construction line for a chord and a separate line with a tangent constraint against the circle. Notice FreeCAD holds the tangent line touching at exactly one point no matter how you drag it — the very fact you'll be asked to prove in Session 3, applied to why a diameter always beats every other chord. Export a screenshot of the constrained sketch for the portfolio.

## Safety
Keep the compass point capped or pointed away from yourself and others when not actively drawing. If a "real circle" you want to measure is a wheel or fan, make sure it is fully stopped and powered off before you measure it — never measure a spinning object.
