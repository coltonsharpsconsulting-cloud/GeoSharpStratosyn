# Theorem Card Wall

*You have used vertical angles, linear pairs, and corresponding/alternate angles all year without ever having to name what makes each one true. Build a permanent, color-coded reference wall — one card per rule, each with its own small diagram — then let a shuffle-and-match round settle whether you actually own the names or just recognize them when they're already labeled.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a mounted six-card reference wall, color-coded postulate vs. theorem, plus a shuffle-and-match log, photographed for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- Six index cards or cut cardstock rectangles (roughly 4"×6" works well)
- A ruler and a fine-tip marker or pen
- Two marker/highlighter colors — one for postulates, one for theorems (a third neutral color for the properties reference card is optional)
- Tape, pins, or a corkboard/binder page to mount the finished wall
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — Build the six cards
Make one card for each rule below. On every card: write the full statement, draw a small diagram showing it, and color the card's border by kind (postulate color or theorem color).

1. **Linear Pair Postulate** — a linear pair is always supplementary. Diagram: two adjacent angles forming a straight line. *Postulate.*
2. **Vertical Angles Theorem** — vertical angles are congruent. Diagram: an X with the vertical pair arc-marked. Note underneath: *"proven from the Linear Pair Postulate."* *Theorem.*
3. **Corresponding Angles Postulate** — if two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, corresponding angles are congruent. Diagram: parallel lines + a transversal, one corresponding pair marked. *Postulate.*
4. **Alternate Interior Angles Theorem** — …then alternate interior angles are congruent. Same diagram, alternate interior pair marked. Note: *"proven from the Vertical Angles Theorem + the Corresponding Angles Postulate."* *Theorem.*
5. **Alternate Exterior Angles Theorem** — …then alternate exterior angles are congruent. Same diagram, alternate exterior pair marked. Same proof-chain note. *Theorem.*
6. **Properties of Equality/Congruence** — one card, three rows: Reflexive (A ≅ A), Symmetric (A ≅ B → B ≅ A), Transitive (A ≅ B and B ≅ C → A ≅ C). Neutral color — this card is a tool, not a claim about angles.

## Part 2 — Mount the wall
7. Mount all six cards where you'll actually see them — a corkboard, a taped strip on the wall by your workspace, or a binder page you'll keep open during proofs.
8. Step back and check: can you tell, by color alone and without reading the text, which cards are postulates and which are theorems?

## Part 3 — Shuffle & Match
9. Turn all six cards face down (or cover the rule names), shuffle them against six blank slips naming just the rule ("Linear Pair Postulate," "Vertical Angles Theorem," etc.), and re-match statement to name from memory.
10. Record how many tries it took (or time yourself), and note which single pair tricked you the most.

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch the crossing-lines figure and the parallel-lines-and-transversal figure as two separate sketches with angle constraints, and add a text annotation on each naming the postulate or theorem it illustrates. Export both as images for two of your six cards instead of hand-drawing them.

## Safety
Craft knives or scissors used to cut cardstock should be pointed away from fingers and closed/capped when not in use. If mounting with pins on a corkboard, keep pin heads facing out and away from where hands will reach.

## Turn in (rubric — 8 pts)
| Criterion | Pts |
|---|---|
| All six cards built, each with a correct statement and a diagram | 2 |
| Postulates and theorems clearly color-coded and correctly sorted | 2 |
| Alternate Interior and Alternate Exterior cards each note the correct proof chain | 1 |
| Wall mounted somewhere visible and usable | 1 |
| Shuffle-and-match log recorded with an honest count/time and a named trick pair | 1 |
| Photo of the mounted wall and the match log | 1 |

Photograph your mounted wall and your match log and drop them in the [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]]. In Session 3 you'll defend, in words, why the Vertical Angles Theorem had to be provable rather than its own postulate — then take the checkpoint.
