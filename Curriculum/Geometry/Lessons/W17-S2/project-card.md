# Volume Hunt & Build — Cones, Pyramids & Spheres

*Find a real cone, a real (or built) pyramid, and something spherical — measure every dimension you need, and compute each one's exact volume. Then flip the proof around: fill a pyramid with rice, sand, or water and pour it into a prism sharing its exact base and height, and watch the ⅓ relationship come out even in your own hands.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a measurement log (cone, pyramid, sphere, volumes computed) plus photos of your ⅓ pouring proof, for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A tape measure or ruler, a pencil, paper, and a calculator
- A piece of string (to find a cone's or sphere's circumference if you don't have calipers — circumference ÷ 2π gives the radius)
- Rice, sand, or water for the pouring proof, plus a tray or tub to catch spills
- Paper, tape, scissors, and a printed net (or FreeCAD) if you're building your own pyramid and matching prism, rather than finding two ready-made
- Three real (or modeled) solids: a cone, a pyramid, and a sphere — pick from an ice-cream cone, a paper party hat, a funnel, a pyramid paperweight, a tent, a tennis ball, a basketball, or an orange
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — Real-Solid Hunt (measure real volumes)
1. Find (or build) a **cone**, a **pyramid**, and something **spherical**.
2. For the cone: measure the **radius** (straight across the base, divided by 2, or by string and circumference ÷ 2π) and the **altitude** — straight up the center, not the slanted side.
3. For the pyramid: measure the **base** (its side length, or its length and width if rectangular) and the **altitude** — straight down from the apex, not the slant height of a face.
4. For the sphere: measure the **radius** the same way as the cone's.
5. Record the shape, its dimensions, the formula you used, and the computed volume, for all three — round any sphere-volume answer to the nearest hundredth.

## Part 2 — The ⅓ Discovery (prove the formula is honest)
6. Gather or build a **pyramid and a prism that share the exact same base and the exact same height** (a square-based paper pyramid and a square-based paper box work well — fold your own net if you don't have matching ones on hand), or a matching **cone and cylinder**.
7. Fill the pyramid (or cone) completely with rice, sand, or water, then pour it into the prism (or cylinder). Repeat.
8. Count how many pours it takes to fill the prism (or cylinder) exactly to the top. If MUS's ⅓ rule is right, the answer should be **three**.
9. If your count is off, recheck: did the pyramid and prism actually share the same base *and* the same height? A mismatch there is the most common reason the pour comes out uneven.

## CAD / 3D-print alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch and pad a pyramid or cone alongside a prism or cylinder sharing its base and height, then use the measurement tool to read both volumes back and confirm the ratio is exactly 3:1. If a 3D printer is available, print both solids and run the rice/sand pour for real. Export a screenshot or photo for the portfolio.

## Safety
Work over a tray, a tub, or a sink when pouring rice, sand, or water — spills happen, and rice underfoot is slippery. Keep water away from anything electronic. Scissors and craft knives cut away from your body, and ask an adult before using a craft knife on cardstock.

## Turn in (rubric — 7 pts)
| Criterion | Pts |
|---|---|
| Cone, pyramid, and sphere all measured correctly (altitude not slant height, radius not diameter, all with units) | 2 |
| Volume computed correctly for all three, using the correct formula for each shape | 2 |
| ⅓ pouring proof completed and reported honestly (matching pyramid/prism or cone/cylinder, pour count given) | 2 |
| Photo of measurement log and/or pouring proof | 1 |

Photograph your measurement log and pouring proof and drop them in the [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]]. In Session 3 you'll defend, in words, why a pyramid always holds exactly ⅓ of a prism sharing its base and height — then take the checkpoint.
