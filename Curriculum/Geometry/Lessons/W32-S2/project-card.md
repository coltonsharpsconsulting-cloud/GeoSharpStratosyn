# Transformation Lab: Slide, Flip, Turn & Tessellate

*Yesterday you moved a triangle on a screen and read its new coordinates off a table. Today you cut out a real shape, predict where it lands BEFORE you move it, then check by actually sliding, flipping, and turning it on graph paper — and finish by using those same three moves, repeated, to tile a page with no gaps, the way Escher did.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a coordinate move log (predicted vs. actual, for a translation and a reflection) plus one hand-drawn tessellation or symmetric design, photographed for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- Graph paper (several sheets)
- Cardstock, cardboard, or a stiff index card to cut your tile from
- Scissors
- Tape (to temporarily anchor a traced position, if helpful)
- A ruler
- Colored pencils or markers
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — Coordinate Move Lab
1. On graph paper, draw and label a set of x- and y-axes. Cut a small triangle or quadrilateral out of cardstock and trace it onto the grid as your **pre-image**, labeling each vertex with its coordinates.
2. Pick a translation vector (h, k). **Before moving the cut-out**, predict every vertex's image coordinates using (x, y) → (x + h, y + k). Write the prediction down.
3. Now physically slide the cut-out piece by (h, k) — count the grid squares by hand — and trace it in its new spot. Read off the actual coordinates and compare to your prediction.
4. Reset the cut-out to the pre-image position. Pick ONE axis (x or y). Predict the reflection's image coordinates using the correct rule, then physically flip the cut-out over that axis (turn it over, matching the mirrored distance on each side) and trace the result. Compare prediction to actual.

## Part 2 — Tessellation / Escher Design
5. Choose one simple tile shape (a square, a triangle, or a custom shape you design by nudging one edge of a square or triangle — the classic Escher trick).
6. Using **only** translations, reflections, and/or rotations of that same tile — never resizing it — cover a region of a fresh sheet of graph paper edge-to-edge with copies of the tile, with no gaps and no overlaps.
7. Color adjacent tiles in alternating colors so the repeating pattern is easy to see.
8. Circle one place on the page where you can point to exactly which transformation (a slide, a flip, or a turn) carries one tile onto the tile right next to it.

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch your tile shape once, then use the sketcher's array/mirror/rotate tools to generate several tiled copies exactly, confirming that no resizing occurred (equal-length constraints on corresponding edges). Export a screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Scissors and cardstock edges can be sharp — cut away from your hand and keep points capped or set down when not in use.

## Turn in (rubric — 8 pts)
| Criterion | Pts |
|---|---|
| Translation predicted BEFORE moving, then physically slid and traced, matching the prediction | 2 |
| Reflection (over one named axis) predicted BEFORE moving, then physically flipped and traced, matching the prediction | 2 |
| Tessellation or symmetric design built using only translations/reflections/rotations, no gaps or overlaps | 2 |
| One tile-to-tile transformation identified and circled on the tessellation | 1 |
| Photo of the coordinate move log and the tessellation | 1 |
