# Angle Hunt & Build

*Find three real angles hiding in your everyday world and measure them like a surveyor would. Then flip the skill around: draw three angles to an exact degree and prove your protractor reading works both ways — measuring and drawing are the same skill, run in opposite directions.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a hunt log (three measured real-world angles) plus a page of three labeled target angles (30°, 90°, 135°), photographed for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A protractor (standard clear plastic, reads 0°–180°)
- A ruler, a sharp pencil, and blank or grid paper
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo
- Three real "hinge-like" spots to measure — pick from: a door you can prop open partway, an analog clock (real or a clock app), a ramp/incline, an open laptop screen, or a pair of open scissors

## Part 1 — Angle Hunt (measure real angles)
1. Find three different angles in the real world. Each one needs a clear **vertex** (the pivot point) and two straight **sides** (the two rays), for example:
   - A door propped open partway — the hinge is the vertex; the door's edge and the frame are the two sides.
   - An analog clock's hour and minute hands at a time you choose — the center of the clock face is the vertex.
   - A ramp, a laptop screen, or open scissors — the pivot point is the vertex.
2. For each one, hold your protractor's center crosshair right at the vertex, line up the **0° line along one side**, and read where the other side crosses the scale — to the nearest whole degree. Watch for the two printed scales; use the one that reads 0° on the side you aligned.
3. Record what the object was, which two rays you measured, and your measured degree, for all three.

## Part 2 — Target Draw (draw exact angles)
4. On a fresh sheet, draw three separate angles at exactly **30°**, **90°**, and **135°**:
   - Draw one ray first — this is your baseline.
   - Put the protractor's center on the ray's endpoint (the vertex), 0° aligned with that ray.
   - Make a small pencil mark at the target degree, on the same scale that read 0° at your baseline.
   - Remove the protractor and draw a straight ray from the vertex through that mark.
5. Label each drawn angle: mark the vertex with a letter, name the angle (e.g. ∠XYZ), and write "m∠XYZ = 90°" beside it.
6. **Check your work.** Lay the protractor back over each drawn angle and confirm it reads within a degree or two of your target.

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch two construction lines from a single shared point and add an **angle constraint**, setting it to 30°, then 90°, then 135° by typing the value instead of eyeballing it. Notice that FreeCAD holds the angle exactly no matter how far you drag the lines — the very fact you'll be asked to prove in Session 3. Export a screenshot of one constrained sketch for the portfolio.

## Safety
Protractors and pencils have points — carry them capped or pointed down. If you're measuring a door, keep fingers clear of the hinge side. Don't force a ramp, scissors, or a laptop hinge past its natural range of motion.
