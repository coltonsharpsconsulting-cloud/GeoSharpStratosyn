# Straw Triangle Rigidity Lab

*You just learned that SSS and SAS are postulates — accepted outright, because three matching sides (or two sides and their included angle) leave no room for a second, different triangle. Words are one thing. Feel it: build a triangle from three fixed straws and try to wiggle it. Build one from two fixed straws and a fixed included angle and try to wiggle that. Then build the same two straws with the angle in the WRONG place and watch it wiggle right in your hands.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** three built triangles (SSS, SAS, SSA), a hinge-test log, photographed for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- Straws, coffee stirrers, or thin dowels (at least 6 — three per triangle, plus spares)
- Scissors and a ruler
- A protractor
- Brads / paper fasteners (or pushpins into a corkboard) so joints can pivot
- Tape or string for backup joints
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — Build the SSS triangle
1. Choose three straw lengths where any two add up to MORE than the third (the triangle inequality) — for example 4", 5", and 6".
2. Cut your straws to those three lengths.
3. Join the three ends into a triangle using a brad at each vertex, so every joint can pivot freely.
4. Grab it anywhere and try to wiggle it. Record what happens.

## Part 2 — Build the SAS triangle
5. Cut two straws to fixed lengths (they can repeat from Part 1 or be new — for example 4" and 6").
6. Join them at one shared vertex with a brad. Use the protractor to fix the angle BETWEEN them at a chosen measure (for example 50°) — tape or brace the joint so that angle can't slide.
7. Close the triangle with a third straw (or a piece of string) of whatever length is needed to reach between the two open ends. Measure and record that length.
8. Grab it anywhere and try to wiggle it. Record what happens.

## Part 3 — The SSA challenge
9. Use the SAME two straw lengths from Part 2, joined at a shared vertex with a brad — but this time set the protractor angle at the OTHER end (not between the two straws).
10. Try to close the triangle with a third piece. Can you find more than one way to close it? Does it wiggle where the SSS and SAS triangles did not?

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch all three cases (SSS, SAS, SSA) with length and angle constraints instead of building by hand. Try dragging a free vertex in the SSA sketch and watch how many valid positions satisfy the same two lengths and one angle — export the sketch as an image alongside your straw photos.

## Safety
Scissors used to cut straws or dowels should be pointed away from fingers and closed when not in use. Brad/paper-fastener points should face away from your palm when pushing them through, and pushpins on a corkboard should be pressed in fully with the point end away from where hands will reach.
