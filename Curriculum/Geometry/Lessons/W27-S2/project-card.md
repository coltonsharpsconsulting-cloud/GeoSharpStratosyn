# Congruence Cutout Lab

*Two triangles cut from the exact same template are congruent whether you believe it or not. Today you prove it the oldest way there is — by picking one up and setting it directly on top of the other — then relabel it, measure it, and write your own correct congruence statement for a correspondence nobody handed you.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** two cutout triangles superimposed to confirm congruence, one relabeled and traced in a new position with all six corresponding parts measured and marked, photographed for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- Cardstock or heavy paper (two same-size sheets, or one sheet folded in half so you can cut both layers at once)
- Scissors
- A ruler
- A protractor
- A pencil
- Three colors of pencil, marker, or highlighter (for matching tick marks and arc marks)
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — Trace & Cut
1. Draw one triangle template — anything scalene works well, since a scalene triangle keeps every side and every angle a different size, so no corresponding part can be confused with another by accident.
2. Trace it a second time (or fold your paper and cut both layers at once) so you end up with **two identical cutouts**.
3. Label the first cutout's three corners **A, B, C**.

## Part 2 — Superimpose Test
4. Pick up cutout #2 and lay it directly on top of cutout #1, corner to corner.
5. Confirm every side and every corner lines up exactly, all the way around. This IS the definition of congruent triangles — seen with your hands, not just stated: if one triangle can be placed exactly on the other, they're congruent.

## Part 3 — Relabel & Measure
6. Pick up cutout #2 again, rotate or flip it into a **new position and orientation** somewhere else on your paper, and trace around it there.
7. Label its three corners **D, E, F** in whatever order YOU choose — but choose on purpose so the order is not simply "whichever corner sits closest to A." (This is exactly the trap from Session 1's concept note: the correspondence is decided by which corner actually superimposed onto which, never by which letters look closest together on the page.)
8. Using your ruler and protractor, measure all three sides and all three angles of BOTH triangles.
9. Mark corresponding parts with matching tick marks (sides) and matching arc marks (angles) — one color per corresponding pair, three colors total.

## Part 4 — Write the Statement
10. Go back to your Part 2 superimpose test and recall exactly which of D, E, F landed on which of A, B, C.
11. Write your own correct congruence statement (for example, △ABC ≅ △DEF, or some other valid order if your corners landed differently) and list all six corresponding pairs — three sides, three angles.

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch one triangle, then copy and rotate the sketch to place a second congruent copy at a new position and orientation. Add length and angle constraints to confirm all six corresponding measurements match exactly, then export a screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Scissors should stay pointed away from fingers and closed when not actively cutting. Keep the protractor's edge and any sharp pencil points away from fingers while marking angles.

## Turn in (rubric — 8 pts)
| Criterion | Pts |
|---|---|
| Two triangles cut from the same template, confirmed to superimpose exactly | 2 |
| Second triangle relabeled D/E/F in an intentionally non-obvious order and traced in a new position | 1 |
| All six corresponding parts measured and correctly tick/arc-marked in matching colors | 3 |
| Correct congruence statement written, matching the actual superimposed correspondence | 1 |
| Photo of the superimposed pair and the labeled trace | 1 |

Photograph your superimposed cutouts and your labeled, measured trace and drop them in the [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]]. In Session 3 you'll defend, in words, why order matters and use CPCTC to conclude a pair of parts equal — then take the checkpoint.
