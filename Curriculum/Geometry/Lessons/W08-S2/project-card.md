# Transversal Hunt & Straightedge Lab

*Draw a real transversal across two real parallel lines and let a protractor settle the question a definition can only assert: do corresponding, alternate interior, and alternate exterior angles really come out congruent? Then go find one more transversal hiding in the world around you. By the end, "the lines are parallel, so the angles match" stops being a rule you were told and becomes something you measured yourself.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a labeled, measured eight-angle diagram plus one real-world transversal find, photographed for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A straightedge or ruler, and a protractor
- Blank or grid paper, a sharp pencil
- Optional: ruled notebook paper (its lines are already parallel) or a rectangular book (its long edges are already parallel)
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo
- A real-world transversal to scout: a crosswalk cutting curb lines, a ladder leaned across two fence rails, a diagonal brace on a fence, blinds or lattice, or a pencil laid across ruled notebook lines

## Part 1 — Draw it
1. Draw two parallel lines. The easiest way: use two of the ruled lines on notebook paper, or trace both long edges of a ruler or book. If you draw them freehand, use your straightedge twice, checking the gap between the lines stays the same at both ends.
2. Lay your straightedge across both lines at a clear slant (not close to 90°, not close to flat) and draw one transversal connecting them.
3. Label the top crossing point **M** and the bottom crossing point **R**. Going clockwise from upper-left at each point, number the four angles: **∠1, ∠2, ∠3, ∠4** at M, and **∠5, ∠6, ∠7, ∠8** at R.

## Part 2 — Measure it
4. Set your protractor's center on M and measure ∠1, ∠2, ∠3, ∠4 to the nearest whole degree. Do the same at R for ∠5, ∠6, ∠7, ∠8. Record all eight in order.
5. **Circle** one corresponding pair (same corner at each crossing, e.g. ∠1 & ∠5). **Box** the two alternate interior pairs (∠3 & ∠6, ∠4 & ∠5). **Underline** the two alternate exterior pairs (∠1 & ∠8, ∠2 & ∠7).

## Part 3 — Confirm the congruences
6. Check every pair you marked. Each one should match within a degree or two of measurement error. Write a one-line note if any pair is off by more than that — did the lines actually stay parallel?

## Part 4 — Transversal Hunt
7. Find ONE real transversal crossing two parallel things in your world — a crosswalk's diagonal curb cut across the parallel stripes, a ladder leaned across two fence rails, a pencil laid across ruled notebook lines.
8. Photograph it. Name which two things are acting as the "parallel lines," name what's acting as the transversal, and name ONE angle pair type (corresponding, alternate interior, or alternate exterior) you can point to.

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch two parallel construction lines (with a parallel constraint) and one transversal crossing both. Add an angle constraint on one angle, then drag the transversal and watch the corresponding, alternate interior, and alternate exterior angles all hold their congruences automatically — exactly what you're about to argue for in Session 3. Export a screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Protractors and pencils have points — carry them capped or pointed down. If your real-world hunt takes you near a crosswalk or a road, stay on the sidewalk, keep clear of traffic, and have an adult with you.

## Turn in (rubric — 8 pts)
| Criterion | Pts |
|---|---|

| All eight angles measured and recorded with a protractor | 2 |
| Corresponding, alternate interior, and alternate exterior pairs each marked and shown to match within tolerance | 2 |
| Real-world transversal found, photographed, and one angle pair type named | 1 |

Photograph your diagram and your real-world find and drop them in the [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]]. In Session 3 you'll defend, in words, why alternate interior angles can never fail to match when the lines are parallel — then take the checkpoint.
