# Equilateral Split Lab

*Every equilateral triangle is secretly hiding two 30-60-90 triangles inside it — cut down the middle. Today you make that cut, measure what falls out, and test whether the 1:√3:2 ratio really holds up in your own hands. If you'd rather skip the scissors, a real 30-60-90 set-square or a 30°/60° ramp works just as well.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a photographed equilateral-triangle split (or measured set-square/ramp) with all three sides recorded and the ratio checked, for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- Cardstock or heavy paper (at least 8 in / 20 cm square)
- A compass and straightedge, or a ruler and protractor, to construct the equilateral triangle
- A ruler marked in a consistent unit (inches or cm)
- A protractor or carpenter's/speed square
- Scissors
- *Alternative:* a real 30-60-90 drafting set-square, or a ramp/roofline built at a 30° or 60° incline
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — Build the Equilateral Triangle & Cut
1. Pick a side length (6 in or 15 cm works well). Construct an equilateral triangle with a compass (swing two arcs of that radius from each end of a base segment and connect their intersection), or measure and check all three sides and angles carefully with a ruler and protractor.
2. Choose one vertex and mark the exact midpoint of the side opposite it.
3. Draw a straight line — the altitude — from that vertex to the midpoint you marked.
4. Cut along the altitude. You now have two congruent right triangles.

## Part 2 — Measure & Test the Ratio
5. On one half-triangle, identify and measure all three sides: the **short leg** (half the original base), the **long leg** (the altitude you drew), and the **hypotenuse** (the original equilateral side).
6. Confirm the right angle sits where the altitude meets the base, using your protractor or square.
7. Compute long leg ÷ short leg (should be near √3 ≈ 1.732) and hypotenuse ÷ short leg (should be near exactly 2). How close did you land?
8. Any gap is almost always the ruler or the cut, not the ratio — the 1:√3:2 relationship is exact; your measuring tools aren't.

## Alternative — Real 30-60-90 Tool
If you have a real 30-60-90 drafting set-square, or can measure a ramp/roofline built at a clean 30° or 60° angle, measure its three sides instead of cutting paper, and run the same two ratio checks.

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch an equilateral triangle with an equal-length constraint on all three sides, add the altitude as a construction line, and let the software's measurement tools confirm the 30-60-90 split and its exact side ratio. Export a screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Cut on a stable surface, away from fingers, blade angled away from your body. If measuring a ramp or ladder for the alternative, don't climb it — measure it where it safely rests.

## Turn in (rubric — 7 pts)
| Criterion | Pts |
|---|---|
| Equilateral triangle constructed and cut exactly along its altitude (or real tool/ramp measured) | 2 |
| All three sides of the resulting 30-60-90 triangle measured and recorded | 2 |
| Long ÷ short and hypotenuse ÷ short ratios computed and compared to √3 and 2 | 2 |
| One sentence naming the hypotenuse correctly before doing the math | 1 |

Photograph your split triangle (or your measured set-square/ramp) and drop it in the [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]]. In Session 3 you'll derive, algebraically, why $1 : \sqrt{3} : 2$ has to hold every time — then take the checkpoint.
