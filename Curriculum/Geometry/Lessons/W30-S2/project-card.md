# Right-Triangle Shortcut Lab

*Yesterday you named HL, LL, HA, and LA on a screen. Today you build all four by hand — fixing only the two named parts each time — and find out whether the rest of the triangle really does land exactly where the math predicts, before you ever measure it.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** four constructed right triangles (one per shortcut) with predictions written next to their measurements, photographed for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- Plain or graph paper (graph paper makes the right angles easier)
- A ruler (cm or in — pick ONE unit and use it for all four)
- A protractor
- A pencil and eraser
- A calculator (or do the arithmetic by hand — either is fine)
- *(Scissors, if you want to trim the page for the portfolio photo)*

## Build it — four constructions, four shortcuts

For each construction, **compute your prediction before you measure anything** — that's the entire point of the lab.

1. **HL (Hypotenuse-Leg).** Draw a right angle at point C. Along one ray, mark leg **CB = 6 units**. Before drawing anything else: compute the other leg CA using the Pythagorean theorem for a hypotenuse of **10 units**. Now find point A for real — either swing a 10-unit arc from B until it crosses the other ray, or use your ruler to measure 10 units from B along a trial line until it lands on the ray. Measure your constructed CA. Does it match your prediction?
2. **LL (Leg-Leg).** Draw a right angle at point C. Mark leg **CB = 5 units** on one ray and leg **CD = 12 units** on the other. Predict the hypotenuse BD with the Pythagorean theorem, THEN connect B to D with a straight segment and measure it.
3. **HA (Hypotenuse-Angle).** Draw hypotenuse **AB = 10 units**. At A, use your protractor to draw a ray at **30°** from AB (this becomes leg AC). At B, protractor a ray at **60°** from BA (this becomes leg BC). Mark C where the two rays cross — the angle at C should come out at 90° on its own, without you ever setting it directly. Predict both legs with trig (opposite ÷ hypotenuse) first, then measure them.
4. **LA (Leg-Angle).** Draw leg **CA = 7 units** from a right-angle vertex C. At A, protractor a ray at **50°** from CA (this becomes the hypotenuse). At C, draw the perpendicular ray (leg CB). Mark B where the rays cross. Predict leg CB and hypotenuse AB with trig first, then measure both.

## Predict, then measure
For each of the four triangles, write your **predicted** value(s) next to your **measured** value(s). A gap of a millimeter or two is just pencil-and-ruler error — the two numbers should land close together every time.

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch each triangle with the two named parts as length/angle constraints and let the software compute the rest exactly — then compare its exact answer to your predicted (by-hand) value. Export one sketch as an image for the portfolio.

## Safety
Keep pencil and protractor points away from fingers when marking angles; if trimming pages with scissors, cut away from your hand.
