# Planes & Sets

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a pencil in hand, drawing each figure and jotting each set as it comes up. Last week you built the four figures out of points. This week you let one of them spread sideways, and you pick up a whole new vocabulary for talking about *groups* of things.*

## The one big question

A line is a one-dimensional string of points. What do you get when you let it spread sideways too — and how do mathematicians talk precisely about a *collection* of things, like "all the points on one flat surface"?

## Planes — geometry gets a second dimension

| Term | Dimensions | What it is | How you write it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Plane** | 2 (length **and** width) | A perfectly flat surface with no thickness, extending forever in every direction. | A single capital letter — plane **M** — or three points not all on one line, e.g. plane **ABC**. |
| **Coplanar** | — | Points or lines that lie in the **same** plane. | — |

- A plane is flat — no bumps, no curves — and it never stops. Picture an infinite tabletop with no edges, stretching in every direction at once.
- **Any three points that are not collinear** pin down exactly one plane. That's why "plane ABC" works as a name: as long as A, B, and C don't all sit on one straight line, there is exactly one flat surface that contains all three.
- **Coplanar** is to planes what collinear is to lines: collinear points share one line; coplanar points (or lines) share one plane.
- **Plane geometry** — the flat, 2D world of this course so far — studies figures that live entirely inside one plane. **Solid geometry** studies figures in three dimensions, where more than one plane is in play. Everything you've drawn so far (points, lines, rays, segments) has quietly been living inside a single plane the whole time.

## Sets — the language for "a group of things"

| Symbol | Name | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| $\{\ \}$ or $\varnothing$ | **empty set** (null set) | A set that contains **nothing at all**. |
| $\cup$ | **union** | Combine everything in set A **and** set B into one group — no double-counting. |
| $\cap$ | **intersection** | Only what's in **both** A and B — the overlap. |
| $\subset$ | **subset** | A group that is **entirely contained inside** another group. |

- A **set** is simply a collection of things — numbers, shapes, points, even household objects. We usually name a set with a capital letter and list its members inside braces: $A = \{2, 4, 6, 8\}$.
- The **empty set** ($\varnothing$) has zero members. It's a perfectly valid set — just an empty one. (A drawer with nothing in it is still a drawer.)
- **Union** ($\cup$) is generous: it grabs everything that belongs to *either* set, combined into one list, with shared members written only once.
- **Intersection** ($\cap$) is strict: it keeps only what shows up in *both* sets. If nothing is shared, $A \cap B = \varnothing$.
- **Subset** ($\subset$) describes containment: if every member of B is also a member of A, then B is a subset of A ($B \subset A$). Squares are a subset of rectangles — every square is a rectangle, but not every rectangle is a square.

## Watch it work

**Numbers.** Let $A = \{2, 4, 6, 8\}$ and $B = \{4, 8, 12\}$.
- $A \cup B = \{2, 4, 6, 8, 12\}$ — every member from either set, each written once.
- $A \cap B = \{4, 8\}$ — only the numbers that appear in *both* lists.
- Neither set is a subset of the other, since each has a member the other lacks (2 and 6 are only in A; 12 is only in B).

**Shapes.** Let $A$ = "quadrilaterals with four right angles" and $B$ = "quadrilaterals with four equal sides." A **square** belongs to both — it's in $A \cap B$. A **rectangle** that isn't a square is in $A$ only. A **rhombus** that isn't a square is in $B$ only. And the set of all squares is a **subset** of both A and B.

**Where lines and planes meet.** These two facts hold every time, and they're worth memorizing cold:
- The **intersection of two lines** (that aren't parallel) is **one point**. Two straight lines can only cross once.
- The **intersection of two planes** (that aren't the same plane) is **one line** — never just a point. Think of two walls meeting: the seam where they touch runs floor to ceiling, a whole line of shared points, not a single dot.
- A plane holds **infinitely many points** — and infinitely many lines — the same "always another one between any two" idea from last week, just spread across two dimensions instead of one.

## Why this matters

Every flat surface you'll ever measure, tile, or build on is a plane, and every time you compare two groups of things — objects, shapes, numbers, requirements — you're doing set work, whether you call it that or not. This week you'll **hunt** for real planes and physically sort real objects into unions and intersections, then **prove** why two planes can only ever meet along a line. Planes give geometry its stage; sets give it its grammar.
