# Perfect-Square Tiles Lab

*A perfect square isn't just a fact to memorize — it's a count of tiles that closes up into a complete square with nothing left over. Today you build several of those squares by hand, then use tiles to physically split a bigger pile into "the biggest square block that fits" plus a remainder — the exact move √72 = 6√2 makes on paper.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a tile log (four perfect-square arrays + three factored radicands) and a completed radical card sort, photographed for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A set of unit tiles, math manipulative squares, or 1-inch graph paper cut into individual squares (at least 100)
- Scissors, a ruler, a marker
- Index cards or slips of paper for the card sort (about 12)
- *(Camera / phone for the portfolio photo)*

## Part 1 — Build the Perfect Squares
1. Using your unit tiles, arrange a **3×3** square. Count the tiles: 9. Do the same for **4×4** (16), **5×5** (25), and **6×6** (36). Record each side length and its tile total — every one of these totals is a **perfect square**, because the tiles close into a complete square with zero left over.
2. Try a **non**-perfect-square count, like 20 tiles. Attempt to arrange them into one complete square. Notice you can't — the biggest complete square you can build uses only 16 of them, with 4 left over in a leftover strip.

## Part 2 — Factor a Radicand With Tiles
3. Count out **72** tiles. Arrange the **largest** complete square block you can build. You should land on a **6×6** block (36 tiles) — arrange the remaining tiles off to the side; there should be exactly 2 left over.
4. That split — a 36-tile block plus a 2-tile remainder — is exactly the step $\sqrt{72} = \sqrt{36 \times 2} = 6\sqrt{2}$. The block's side length (6) becomes the coefficient out front; the leftover count (2) stays under the radical.
5. Repeat the same physical factoring for **48** (you should land on a 4×4 block, 16 tiles, with 3 left over → $4\sqrt{3}$) and **50** (a 5×5 block, 25 tiles, with 2 left over → $5\sqrt{2}$).

## Part 3 — Radical Card Sort
6. On separate index cards, write six radicals: $\sqrt{12}$, $\sqrt{18}$, $\sqrt{20}$, $\sqrt{32}$, $\sqrt{45}$, $\sqrt{72}$. On six more cards, write their simplified forms, shuffled out of order: $2\sqrt{3}$, $3\sqrt{2}$, $2\sqrt{5}$, $4\sqrt{2}$, $3\sqrt{5}$, $6\sqrt{2}$.
7. Shuffle both piles together and match each radical card to its simplified-form card. For each match, say out loud which perfect square you pulled out and how you know it's the *largest* one available.

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch an n×n grid of unit squares (an array/pattern of a single square sketch) to build a 6×6 block, then add a small unconstrained strip alongside it representing the remainder — a digital version of the tile split for 72. Export a screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Scissors are sharp if you're cutting your own tiles — cut away from your fingers, and keep the blade closed when not in use.
