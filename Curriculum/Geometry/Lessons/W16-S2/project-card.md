# Volume Hunt & Build

*Find real containers — a cereal box, a soup can, a room — measure every dimension you need, and compute each one's exact volume. Then flip the proof around: verify at least one answer by water displacement or by packing it with unit cubes, or model and 3D-print a solid in FreeCAD and read its volume back.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a measurement log (three containers, at least one rectangular solid and one cylinder, volumes computed) plus your verification setup or CAD/print photos, for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A tape measure or ruler, a pencil, paper, and a calculator
- A piece of string (to find a can's circumference if you don't have calipers — circumference ÷ 2π gives the radius)
- A graduated measuring cup or container and water (for the displacement path), and/or unit cubes — sugar cubes, wooden blocks, LEGO bricks, or 3D-printed 1" cubes
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo
- Three real containers to measure — pick from: a cereal box, a moving box, a shoebox, a soup can, a candle, a water bottle, a bedroom closet, or a room

## Part 1 — Real-Container Hunt (measure real volumes)
1. Find three containers: a **rectangular box**, a **cylinder**, and one more solid (a room, a closet, or a second box).
2. For each rectangular solid: measure **length, width, and height** with a tape measure or ruler — make sure every measurement runs along an edge, not a diagonal.
3. For the cylinder: measure the **height**, then find the **radius** — either measure straight across the circular base (the diameter) and divide by 2, or wrap a string around it, measure the circumference, and divide by 2π.
4. Record the shape, its dimensions, the formula you used, and the computed volume, for all three.

## Part 2 — Verify (prove the formula is honest)
5. Pick one container and confirm its volume a second way:
   - **Water displacement** — submerge a small solid object in a marked container of water and read how much the water level rises (that rise, in mL or cm³, is the object's volume) — or fill a hollow container (like the soup can) to the brim, pour the water into a measuring cup, and read its volume directly.
   - **Unit cubes** — pack a small box with same-size cubes (sugar cubes, wooden blocks, 1" 3D-printed cubes) and count how many fill it completely.
6. Compare your two answers. If they don't match, recheck which dimension you measured — mixing up **radius and diameter** is the single most common cylinder mistake.

## CAD / 3D-print alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch and pad (extrude) a rectangular solid or cylinder with dimensioned constraints, then use the measurement tool to read its volume back and confirm it matches your hand computation. If a 3D printer is available, print your solid and verify by water displacement or by weighing it against a material of known density. Export a screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Tape measures can snap back — retract slowly, not with a flick. Don't climb or stretch to measure anything overhead or unstable (a high shelf, a ceiling) — measure what you can reach safely, or ask an adult. For water displacement, work over a sink or a tray in case of spills, and keep water away from anything electronic.
