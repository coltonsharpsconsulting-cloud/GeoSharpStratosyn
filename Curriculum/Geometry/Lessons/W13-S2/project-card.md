# Regular Polygon Hunt & Angle-Sum Lab

*A stop sign, a hex nut, a floor tile — the world is full of regular polygons that were cut to an exact angle before they could close up perfectly. Today you hunt down (or build) four of them, predict their diagonal counts and angle sums BEFORE you check, then construct one regular polygon from nothing but a compass and a straightedge.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a completed hunt log (four regular polygons, predicted and measured) plus one compass-constructed regular hexagon, photographed for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A stop sign (a photo, a drawing, or a toy/road-sign model)
- A hex nut, bolt head, or hexagonal pencil
- A square floor tile, ceiling tile, or checkerboard square
- A pentagon-shaped object (a paving stone, a pattern block, a home-plate-style shape, or a drawn pentagon)
- A ruler or tape measure
- A protractor
- A compass and a straightedge (a ruler works)
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — Hunt & Predict
1. Find or gather at least **four** real regular (or near-regular) polygons — start from the materials list above, or substitute any regular polygon you can find around the house (a soccer-ball panel, a snowflake pattern, a stained-glass tile).
2. For EACH one, BEFORE measuring anything, **predict**:
   - The number of sides, $n$, and the polygon's name.
   - Diagonals from one vertex: $n - 3$.
   - Interior angle sum: $(n-2) \times 180°$.
   - Each interior angle (since it's regular): sum $\div\, n$.
3. Now **verify**: count the actual sides (and diagonals, if you can trace them), and measure at least one interior angle with a protractor. Does it match your prediction, within a few degrees?

## Part 2 — Compass & Straightedge Construction
4. Using your compass, draw a circle of any radius on a blank sheet.
5. Without changing the compass width, plant the point anywhere ON the circle and swing a small arc that crosses the circle — mark that crossing point.
6. Move the compass point to that new mark (compass width still unchanged) and swing again, marking the next crossing point. Repeat all the way around the circle. Because the compass width never changes, the radius steps around the circle **exactly six times**, landing back where you started.
7. Connect the six marked points in order with your straightedge. You've just constructed a **regular hexagon** — using nothing but a compass and a straightedge, no ruler measurement needed after the first circle.
8. Check your work: measure all six sides with a ruler (they should equal the circle's radius) and one interior angle with a protractor (it should read about 120°).

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch a circle and use an equal-radius circle constraint stepped six times around it to locate the hexagon's vertices exactly the way the physical compass does — then connect the six points and add an equal-length constraint on all six sides to confirm it's regular. Export a screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Compass points are sharp — keep the point capped or away from fingers when not swinging an arc, and carry it point-down.
