# Circles: Parts & Vocabulary

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a compass and ruler in hand, sketching each part as it comes up. For the last several weeks you've closed shapes out of straight sides — polygons, their diagonals, their interior angles. This week the straight sides disappear: every point curves away from one single center, and a whole new vocabulary describes the pieces.*

## The one big question

A polygon closes with straight sides you can count and corners you can point to. A circle has no straight sides and no corners at all — so how do you even talk about "a piece" of one, or compare one straight cut across it to another?

## Anatomy of a circle

| Part | What it is | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Center** | The one point that sits the same distance from every point on the circle. | point C |
| **Radius** | A segment from the center to any point on the circle. Every radius of the *same* circle is congruent — equal length, no matter which point you draw it to. | segment CA |
| **Diameter** | A chord that passes straight through the center. Always exactly **twice the radius** ($d = 2r$), and the longest chord the circle has. | segment AB, through C |
| **Chord** | A segment with *both* endpoints on the circle — no requirement that it pass through the center. | segment AD |
| **Secant** | A full **line** (not just a segment) that intersects the circle at exactly **two** points. | line AD, extended both ways |
| **Tangent** | A line that touches the circle at exactly **one** point — and at that point, it is always **perpendicular** to the radius drawn there. | a line grazing the circle at A only |
| **Arc** | A piece of the circle's circumference. The shorter piece is the **minor arc**, the longer piece the **major arc** — together they always total 360°. | arc AD |
| **Sector** | A piece of the circle's *area* — the pie-slice region boxed in by two radii and the arc between their endpoints. | sector CAD |
| **Circumference** | The perimeter of the circle — the distance all the way around it. | — |

## Chord, secant, or tangent — count the points

These three are easy to mix up because they all involve a straight line meeting a curved circle, so sort them by **how many points** they touch:
- A **chord** is just a *segment* — two endpoints, both on the circle, nothing beyond them.
- A **secant** stretches that same idea into a full *line* — it still crosses the circle at exactly **two** points, but it keeps going past them in both directions.
- A **tangent** is a line that touches the circle at exactly **one** point and no more. At that single point of contact, a tangent is always perpendicular to the circle's radius — lean it any other way and it would have to cut across and hit a second point, making it a secant instead.

## Arcs, sectors, and the 360° that never changes

Pick any two points on a circle and they split the circumference into two arcs — a **minor arc** (the shorter way around) and a **major arc** (the longer way around) — and no matter where those two points sit, minor + major always adds up to exactly **360°**. An arc that measures *exactly* 180° — precisely half the circle — has its own name: a **semicircle**. Drop the two radii to those same two points and you've boxed in a **sector**: not a piece of the perimeter like an arc, but a piece of the whole circular *area*, shaped like a wedge of pie.

## Two shapes that borrow the word "circle"

A **sphere** is what you get when a circle stops being flat — a three-dimensional circle, every point in *space* the same distance from a center (think of a ball, or a globe). An **ellipse** is the opposite kind of stretch: a circle pulled long in one direction — an oval, like a planet's orbit or the shape of most race tracks. Neither one is a circle, but both only make sense once you know exactly what a circle is.

## Watch it work

**The congruent-radii check.** Draw a circle with a compass, then draw three different radii to three different points on the rim. Measure all three with a ruler. Every single time, they come out exactly equal — because "the same distance from the center" is *the definition* of a circle, not a coincidence.

**The longest-chord check.** Draw a circle and mark two points on it that are close together — the chord between them is short. Now slide one point around until the chord swings through the center. Measure again: it's now as long as it can possibly get, exactly equal to the diameter. No chord that misses the center can ever catch up to one that runs straight through it — you'll be asked to argue exactly why in Session 3.

## Why this matters

A bicycle wheel's spokes are radii; its rim's own curve is an arc of its circumference; a pizza cutter carves out sectors; a tightrope strung across a circular fountain is a chord; a car's tire touches the road at a single tangent point with every rotation. This week you'll **name** every one of these parts on sight, **build** or hunt them in the real world, and then **prove** why a diameter always beats every other chord and why a circle's radii never disagree with each other. Master this vocabulary now, and next week's lesson — circumference and area — hangs a formula on every part you meet here.
