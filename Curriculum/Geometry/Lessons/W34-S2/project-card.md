# Height & Distance Capstone Lab

*Yesterday you flipped fractions on a screen. Today you build a real clinometer, sight a real object you cannot climb up and measure, and use trig to find its height — then turn your own measurements into all six trig ratios, reduced. This is the capstone of everything SOHCAHTOA and its reciprocals were for.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** one completed height/distance calculation with all six trig ratios computed from your own measurements, photographed for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A protractor and a plastic drinking straw
- Tape (to fix the straw along the protractor's straight edge)
- A piece of string (about 20 cm) and a small washer, nut, or paperweight to act as a plumb weight
- A long tape measure or a trundle wheel / measured string for the baseline
- A calculator
- A partner to help sight and read the string
- Pencil and paper

## Build it — the clinometer

1. Tape the straw flat along the flat (straight) edge of the protractor, so sighting through the straw means sighting along the 0°–180° line.
2. Tie the string through the small hole at the protractor's center (its pivot point) and let the weight hang freely below it. When you hold the straw level (horizontal), the string should hang straight down, crossing the scale at **90°**.

## Measure it — a real height, end to end

3. **Pick a tall target** you cannot climb to measure directly: a flagpole, a basketball hoop, a tall tree, a roofline, or a doorway.
4. **Measure the baseline.** Stand at a point on the ground and measure the straight-line distance from that point to directly below (or in front of) the target. Record it: this is your triangle's **adjacent** side.
5. **Measure your eye height** — the height of the straw at your eye, off the ground, while standing at that point.
6. **Sight the target.** Hold the clinometer so you look through the straw straight at the *top* of the target. Have your partner read where the weighted string now crosses the protractor's scale.
7. **Find the angle of elevation.** Because the string reads 90° when level, your angle of elevation θ = |reading − 90°|.

## Solve the real problem — then find all six ratios

8. **Compute the height above your eye:** height-above-eye = baseline × tan θ. **Add your eye height** to get the target's total height off the ground.
9. **Compute the hypotenuse** — your line-of-sight distance to the top — with the Pythagorean theorem: hyp = √(baseline² + height-above-eye²).
10. You now know all **three sides** of a real, physically measured right triangle: adjacent (baseline), opposite (height-above-eye), and hypotenuse (line-of-sight). Compute **all six trigonometric ratios** for θ from these three numbers — sin, cos, tan, csc, sec, cot — reducing fractions where the numbers allow you to, and also writing each as a decimal.
11. **Check your reciprocals:** multiply sin θ × csc θ, cos θ × sec θ, and tan θ × cot θ. Each product should land at (or extremely close to) **1**.

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch your triangle with the measured baseline and angle as constraints and let the software compute the height and hypotenuse exactly — then compare its answer to your by-hand trig calculation. Export a screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Work outdoors carefully: watch your footing while walking the baseline and looking upward, don't back into traffic or obstacles, and never sight anywhere near the sun. Keep the weighted string clear of faces when it's swinging.
