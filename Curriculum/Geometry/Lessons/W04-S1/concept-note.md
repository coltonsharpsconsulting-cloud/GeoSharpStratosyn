# Angles: Measuring & Drawing

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a pencil and a protractor in hand, measuring each figure as it comes up. Last week a line spread sideways into a plane. This week two rays leave the same point and tilt apart, and you learn to put one exact number on how far.*

## The one big question

A ray can point in any direction. If two rays share a starting point but lean away from each other, how do you say — in one exact number — how far they've turned apart?

## Anatomy of an angle

| Part | What it is | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Vertex** | The single shared endpoint where both rays start. | point B |
| **Sides** | The two rays that form the angle. | ray BA, ray BC |
| **Angle** | The figure made by two rays sharing a vertex — the "opening" between them. | ∠ABC |
| **m∠ABC** | The *measure* of the angle — how many degrees wide that opening is. | m∠ABC = 42° |

An **angle** is formed by two rays that share a common endpoint, called the **vertex**. The rays themselves are the angle's **sides**. Nothing about an angle is measured in length — an angle measures a *turn*, not a distance.

## Naming an angle — three ways

- **Three letters, vertex in the middle:** ∠ABC or ∠CBA — both name the *same* angle, because the vertex (B) sits in the middle either way. Get the order wrong (vertex not in the middle) and you've named a different angle, or nothing at all.
- **One letter:** ∠B — quick and legal, but *only* if B is the vertex of just one angle in the figure. If two or more angles share vertex B, one letter is ambiguous and you must use three.
- **A number:** ∠1 — a label written directly in a diagram, used the same way when several angles crowd one vertex.

## Degrees, and the tool that reads them

Angles are measured in **degrees** (°), on a scale from **0° to 360°** — once all the way around a full circle. A standard protractor, though, is only a half-circle, so it *reads* 0° to 180°.

Two rays that point in exactly opposite directions from the same vertex — together forming one straight line — make a **straight angle**, measuring exactly **180°**. Turn a quarter of that and you get a **right angle**, exactly **90°** (you'll meet the full family of angle names next lesson — for now, just recognize 90° and 180° on sight).

## Reading a protractor — measuring to the nearest degree

1. Set the protractor's center mark (the little crosshair or hole) exactly on the vertex.
2. Rotate the protractor so its **0° line lies right along one side (ray)** of the angle — this is your baseline.
3. Follow the *other* ray out to where it crosses the curved scale. Read the number, to the nearest whole degree.
4. **Watch for two scales.** Most protractors print two rows of numbers — one running 0°→180° left to right, the other 0°→180° right to left. Use whichever scale reads **0° on the ray you aligned in step 2**; the other scale will show 180° there instead, and reading the wrong one is the single most common protractor mistake.

## Drawing an angle of a given measure

Say you need to draw m∠XYZ = 75°:
1. Draw one ray first — this is your baseline (say, ray YX).
2. Place the protractor's center on Y (the vertex), with 0° aligned along ray YX.
3. Find 75° on the *same scale* that read 0° at YX, and make a small pencil mark there.
4. Remove the protractor and draw a straight ray from Y through that mark. That new ray is YZ, and ∠XYZ now measures exactly 75°.

## Watch it work

**Same rays, longer or shorter.** Draw ∠ABC = 40°. Now erase ray BC and redraw it twice as long, same direction. Measure again — still 40°. Length changed; the *turn* didn't. That's the whole idea behind an angle's measure, and it's exactly what you'll be asked to defend in Session 3.

**The straight-angle check.** Draw any ray BA, then extend it straight through B to a point D on the opposite side. ∠ABD is a straight angle — no protractor needed to know it's 180°, because BA and BD are opposite rays forming one unbroken line.

## Why this matters

A door swinging on its hinge, a ramp meeting the ground, a clock's hands at any given minute, a roofline against a wall — every one of them is two rays sharing a vertex, and every one of them has an exact degree measure whether or not anyone ever checks it with a protractor. This week you'll **measure** angles hiding in your everyday world and **draw** a set of exact target angles by hand, then **prove** why the rays' length never changes the answer. Master the protractor now, and every triangle, polygon, and trig ratio later this year leans on it.
