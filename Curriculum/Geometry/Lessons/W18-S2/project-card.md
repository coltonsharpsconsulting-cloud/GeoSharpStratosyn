# Surface Area Hunt & Build

*Unfold a real box and a real can into their nets — or design and fold your own — measure every face, and compute exactly how much material each one needs.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a measurement log (a rectangular solid and a cylinder, both unfolded into nets, every face measured, surface area computed) plus your verification and/or CAD/fold photos, for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- A ruler or tape measure, scissors, pencil, paper, and a calculator
- An empty cereal box, shoebox, or small cardboard box you can cut open
- An empty can (soup, vegetable, etc.) with a paper label you can remove or trace, or a strip of paper and tape to wrap around it and mark its circumference
- Graph paper or plain paper (for drawing a net to scale), and/or FreeCAD if designing digitally
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — Real-Container Net Hunt (unfold and measure)
1. Choose a **rectangular box** and an empty **can**.
2. Carefully cut along the edges of the box (or trace its faces before cutting) until it lies completely flat with all six faces connected and visible — no face cut into pieces.
3. Measure each face's length and width with a ruler, and label which face is which (top, bottom, front, back, two sides).
4. Peel or cut the can's label off and lay it flat — it should already be a rectangle. Measure its height and its width (that width *is* the base circle's circumference). Trace the top or bottom circle and measure its radius (straight across the circle through its center, divided by 2 — or measure the circumference with a string and divide by 2π).
5. Compute each solid's total surface area from your measured net, showing every face's area and the sum, in square units.

## Part 2 — Verify (prove the unfolding is honest)
6. For the box: add up your six measured face areas and compare that sum to $2(lw+lh+wh)$ computed from the box's outer length, width, and height — they should match.
7. For the can: confirm the label's measured width equals $2\pi r$ using your measured radius (within measuring tolerance) — this is the entire reason a cylinder's lateral area formula works.
8. If your numbers don't match, recheck which measurement is which — mixing up a face's two sides, or the can's radius and diameter, is the single most common mistake.

## CAD / paper-net alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch a net (six connected rectangles for a box, or two circles plus a rectangle for a cylinder) with dimensioned constraints, then fold or assemble it — physically print and fold it, or confirm on-screen that the edges line up — and check the total area matches your hand computation. On paper, drawing a net to scale and folding it into an actual 3-D box or tube works just as well. Export a photo or screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Scissors and box cutters are sharp — cut away from your hand and fingers, and ask an adult for help opening tough cardboard or cutting open a metal can (watch for sharp edges on the cut rim). Work over a table, not your lap.

| Criterion | Pts |

| Photo of the unfolded net(s) and/or verification setup | 1 |
