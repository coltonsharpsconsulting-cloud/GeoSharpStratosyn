# Straw Triangle Lab

*Some sets of three lengths snap together into a perfect triangle. Others never will, no matter how you angle them. Today you find out which is which with your own hands — then build one triangle from nothing but a compass and a straightedge, the oldest tools in geometry.*

**Time:** ~1 hour · **Deliverable:** a completed test log (five length trios, predicted and actual) plus one compass-constructed equilateral triangle, photographed for your [[Records/Portfolio|Portfolio]].

## Materials
- Drinking straws, wooden skewers, or craft sticks you can cut to length (or a bag of pre-cut lengths)
- A ruler or tape measure, marked in the same units you cut to (inches or centimeters)
- Tape, pipe cleaners, twist-ties, or brass fasteners to join the ends at each vertex
- A protractor
- A compass and a straightedge (a ruler works)
- Camera / phone for the portfolio photo

## Part 1 — Straw Test (the triangle inequality, by hand)
1. Cut (or gather) straws/sticks at these lengths, all in the same unit: **3, 4, 4, 5, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 12, 15**. You'll pull five trios from this set.
2. Test these five trios, in order. For EACH one, first **predict yes or no** using the triangle inequality (add the two shorter lengths — is the sum more than the longest?) and write your prediction down **before** you touch the straws:
   - 3, 4, 5
   - 4, 4, 4
   - 5, 5, 9
   - 5, 7, 15
   - 6, 8, 10
3. Now physically join each trio end-to-end at three shared vertices (tape, a pipe cleaner, or a fastener at each corner) and try to close it into a triangle. Record whether it actually closed, right next to your prediction.
4. For every trio that DID close, measure its three angles with a protractor and classify it BOTH ways — by its sides (scalene / isosceles / equilateral) and by its angles (acute / right / obtuse).

## Part 2 — Compass & Straightedge Construction
5. On a blank sheet, draw one straight segment with your straightedge — this will be one side of your triangle.
6. Set your compass's width to exactly that segment's length (point on one endpoint, pencil on the other).
7. Without changing the compass width, plant the point on **one** endpoint of your segment and swing a wide arc above it. Do the same from the **other** endpoint. The two arcs cross at one point.
8. Connect that crossing point back to both original endpoints with your straightedge. You've just constructed an **equilateral triangle** — using nothing but a compass and a straightedge, no ruler measurement needed after the first segment.
9. Check your work: measure all three sides with a ruler. Do they match?

## CAD alternative (or extension)
If FreeCAD is set up, sketch a segment and use two circle constraints of equal radius centered on its endpoints to locate the third vertex exactly the way the physical compass does — then connect the three points and add an equal-length constraint on all three sides to confirm equilateral. Export a screenshot for the portfolio.

## Safety
Straw/stick ends and compass points are sharp — keep the compass point away from fingers and capped when not swinging an arc, and keep stick ends pointed down while carrying the board.
