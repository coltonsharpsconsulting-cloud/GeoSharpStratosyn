# Types of Angles

*This is your read for the week. Take ten quiet minutes with it before our first session — a pencil and a protractor in hand, sorting each measure into its family as it comes up. Last week you learned to put one exact number on an angle. This week that number earns a name.*

## The one big question

Once you can measure an angle to the exact degree, how do you say — in one word — what *kind* of angle it is? And what happens when two angles get added together?

## The five families, sorted by degree

| Type | Degree range | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **Acute** | greater than 0° and less than 90° | A narrow, "tight" opening — sharper than a square corner. |
| **Right** | **exactly** 90° | A perfect square corner — marked with a small square symbol, never an arc. |
| **Obtuse** | greater than 90° and less than 180° | Wider than square, but not yet flat. |
| **Straight** | **exactly** 180° | The two rays point in exactly opposite directions — one unbroken line. |
| **Reflex** | greater than 180° and less than 360° | Bigger than straight — the "far side" of an angle, curving past flat and back around. |

Read that table twice. Notice that only **two** of the five values are *exact* single numbers (90° and 180°) — the other three are *ranges*, and every range is **strict**: it never touches its own boundary.

- **Acute** angles are small — anywhere from just above nothing to just under a right angle.
- A **right** angle is the one exact stopping point at 90°. It gets its own name and its own mark (a little square at the vertex) because it's the single boundary between "acute" and "obtuse" — it is neither.
- **Obtuse** angles pick up right where right angles leave off, and keep going until just under a straight line.
- A **straight** angle is two opposite rays forming one line — exactly 180°, no more, no less.
- **Reflex** angles are the biggest family: everything wider than straight, all the way up to (but never touching) a full 360° turn back to the start.

> **The boundary test.** Ask "does this measure land exactly on 90° or 180°, or does it fall strictly between two of the landmarks?" Landing exactly on 90° or 180° gives you **right** or **straight** — nothing else. Falling strictly between two landmarks (0°–90°, 90°–180°, or 180°–360°) gives you **acute**, **obtuse**, or **reflex**. A measure of *exactly* 90° is never "acute," even if it's 89.9° away from being one — the boundary belongs entirely to "right."

## Naming from a single vertex — there are always two

Math-U-See's Lesson 4 makes a point worth remembering: any two rays sharing a vertex actually define **two** angles at once — the "short way around" and the "long way around" — and together those two measures always add to 360°. Unless a problem tells you otherwise, **assume the smaller angle is meant.** But the larger one is real too, and it's always the **reflex** counterpart of the smaller one (as long as the smaller one is less than 180°).

## Watch it work — adding angle types together

When two angles share a ray and sit side by side, their measures simply **add** — and the sum tells you the new type:

- A **right** angle (90°) + an **acute** angle (say, 20°) = **110°** → that's **obtuse**.
- A **right** angle (90°) + a **right** angle (90°) = **180°** → that's **straight**.
- A **straight** angle (180°) + an **acute** angle (say, 15°) = **195°** → that's **reflex**.

The rule never changes: add the degree numbers first, *then* reclassify the total using the same five ranges from the table above. It doesn't matter which two types you start with — the sum is just a new number, and that number has its own honest name.

## Watch it work — the boundary itself

Draw any angle at 89°. It's acute. Now nudge one ray until the angle reads exactly 90° — it instantly becomes a right angle, not "an acute angle that got lucky." Nudge it one more degree, to 91°, and it's obtuse. The type never blends; it snaps from one family to the next at the exact boundary. That snap — not a gradual fade — is what you'll be asked to defend in Session 3.

## Why this matters

A door swinging open passes through all five families in one motion: closed is 0° (no angle yet), a crack open is acute, squared away from the wall is right, wider is obtuse, flat against an adjoining wall is straight — and a hinge strong enough to keep going past that traces a reflex angle on its way back around. Ramps, scissors, clock hands, and roof pitches all live somewhere on this same five-family scale, whether or not anyone ever names them. This week you'll **hunt and build** real examples of each type — including the trickiest one, reflex — then **prove** why the boundaries never blur and why the addition rule always works.
