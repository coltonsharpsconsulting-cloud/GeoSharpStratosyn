# Polygons: Angles, Diagonals & Regular Polygons

Look at a stop sign. Eight straight sides, closed up into one figure, every angle exactly equal — that makes it a **regular octagon**. You already know a triangle's angles sum to 180° and a quadrilateral's sum to 360° (Lesson 8's diagonal split it into two triangles to prove it). This week you find the one formula that predicts the angle sum for a shape with ANY number of sides, no measuring required — and meet the "regular" polygons where you can even name a single interior angle instead of just a total.

The trick is the same one Lesson 8 already showed you, just scaled up: draw every diagonal from ONE corner of a polygon, and it always splits cleanly into triangles — exactly two fewer triangles than the polygon has sides.

## Words to own
- **Polygon** — any closed, flat figure made of straight sides, named by its number of sides: pentagon (5), hexagon (6), heptagon (7), octagon (8), nonagon (9), decagon (10), undecagon (11), dodecagon (12) — past that, just say the number: a 15-gon.
- **Diagonal** — a segment joining two NON-adjacent vertices. From one vertex of an n-sided polygon: n − 3 diagonals, splitting it into n − 2 triangles.
- **Interior angle sum** — (n − 2) × 180°, for a polygon with n sides.
- **Regular polygon** — all sides congruent AND all angles congruent; each interior angle = (n − 2) × 180° ÷ n.
- **Exterior angle** — always adds up to 360° total around any convex polygon, no matter how many sides; each exterior angle of a regular polygon = 360° ÷ n.

## Build-log prompt (bring an answer)
Find one real regular (or near-regular) polygon around your house — a stop sign, a hex nut, a floor tile, a pentagon-shaped paver. Count its sides, then **predict**, before you measure anything, its interior angle sum and each interior angle using the formulas above. Sketch it in your Compass and note whether a protractor check would confirm your prediction.
