# Area of Parallelograms, Triangles & Trapezoids

Look at a tile on a floor, a triangular flag on a pole, a tabletop that flares wider at one end. Each one covers some amount of surface — and for each one, that amount has an exact number, found the same way every time: multiply a **base** by a **height** that meets it at a perfect right angle.

A rectangle just multiplies its two sides. A parallelogram does the same thing, once you know its slanted side isn't the height. A triangle is always exactly half of that same rectangle. A trapezoid averages its two parallel bases first, then multiplies by the height.

## Words to own
- **Base** — the side you measure along; **Height** — the perpendicular (⊥) distance from that base to the opposite side or point. Never the slanted side.
- **Parallelogram area** — $A = b \cdot h$. **Triangle area** — $A = \tfrac12 b \cdot h$, always half a same-base-same-height rectangle. **Trapezoid area** — $A = \frac{b_1+b_2}{2}\cdot h$, the average of the two bases times the height.
- **Composite figure** — a shape built from simpler shapes; find its area by splitting it apart and adding the pieces.

## Build-log prompt (bring an answer)
Find **one flat surface at home** that is a parallelogram, a triangle, or a trapezoid — not a plain rectangle (a roof gable, a pennant, a tabletop, a paver, a slice of something). Sketch it in your Compass, mark a base and its perpendicular height, and guess its area before you measure.
