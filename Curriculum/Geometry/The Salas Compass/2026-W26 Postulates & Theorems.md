# Postulates & Theorems

You've called "vertical angles are congruent" a rule all year without ever asking *how we know*. This week draws the line: some geometry facts are simply **agreed to** (postulates), and everything else is **proven** from them, one justified step at a time (theorems). The Vertical Angles Theorem you've trusted since Lesson 6 turns out to be provable from just one earlier postulate — and next unit you'll write that proof yourself, formally, in two columns.

## Words to own
- **Postulate / axiom** — accepted true without proof; a starting rule.
- **Theorem** — proven true using postulates, definitions, and earlier theorems.
- **Corollary** — a near-free conclusion that follows right from a theorem.
- **Converse** — a statement with its hypothesis and conclusion swapped; not automatically true.
- **Linear Pair Postulate** (assumed) and **Vertical Angles Theorem** (proven from it) — the pair at the center of this lesson.
- **Corresponding Angles Postulate** (assumed) and the **Alternate Interior/Exterior Angles Theorems** (proven from it).

## Build-log prompt (bring an answer)
Before Session 1, try to prove it yourself: two lines cross at a point, making four angles in order. Using only "a linear pair is supplementary," argue why the two angles directly across from each other must be equal. Write your argument in your Compass — even a rough version. You'll compare it to the formal version in Session 1.
