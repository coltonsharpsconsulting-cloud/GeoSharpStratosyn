# Surface Area of Solids

Look around: a cereal box, a soup can, a shipping box. Each one has an exact amount of *outside* — and you find it the same way every time: cut the solid open along its edges, lay it flat, and add up the areas of every face you see.

That flattened pattern is called a **net**. A rectangular solid's net is six rectangles in three matching pairs. A cylinder's net is two circles plus one rectangle — the curved side, unrolled — whose width has to be the circle's circumference, so it wraps back around exactly once.

## Words to own
- **Total surface area** — the sum of the areas of *all* a solid's faces, in square units. **Net** — the solid, cut along its edges and unfolded completely flat.
- **Rectangular solid** — $SA = 2(lw + lh + wh)$. **Cylinder** — $SA = 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi r h$, two circles plus the unrolled rectangle.
- **Lateral area** — the side faces only, no bases; **total** surface area always adds the base(s) back in.

## Build-log prompt (bring an answer)
Find **one container at home** that's a box or a can — sketch its net in your Compass (unfold it in your head, or actually cut one open), mark every face, and guess its total surface area before you measure and compute it.
