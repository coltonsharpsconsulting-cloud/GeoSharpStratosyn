# Capstone Build

This is the last build of the year, and it's meant to hold all of it at once. Unit 01 taught you to measure a flat figure and prove your perimeter and angles. Unit 02 taught you to fill a solid with volume and wrap it in surface area. Unit 03 taught you to reach a height you couldn't touch with trigonometry, and to move a figure on the coordinate plane and prove it stayed rigid. The capstone asks for all three in one project: a real trig measurement, a built or printed solid, and a transformation — written up with a short proof, the way a mathematician actually closes an argument.

Session 1 is planning — choose your object to measure, your solid to build, and your figure to transform, and write the plan down before you touch a ruler. Session 2 is Build Day — make all three real, and record every number as you go, because nothing counts until it's measured. Session 3 is the write-up — turn your three parts into a portfolio entry someone else could read and believe, closing with a short proof that your transformation preserved (or predictably changed) length and angle.

## Words to own
- **Angle of elevation** — the angle up from level ground to something you're sighting, the starting point for a trig height measurement.
- **Rigid transformation** — a translation, reflection, or rotation; preserves every side length and angle. A **dilation** does not — it scales length by a constant ratio while preserving angle.
- **Portfolio** — the [[Records/Portfolio]] page where this build's photo/CAD file and write-up will live as transcript evidence.

## Before Session 1 (bring an idea)
Start scouting now: what's one real object near you tall or far enough that you'd need an angle and trig to measure it? What's one solid you could build or print that means something to you (a container, a piece, a model)? Come with a rough idea for all three parts so Session 1 is spent refining a plan, not inventing one from nothing.

> This is the year-end capstone — the goal is a finished, provable portfolio entry that stands as the clearest evidence of what you learned this year.
