# Rationalizing Denominators

A calculator says $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt2} \approx 0.7071$ and $\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2} \approx 0.7071$ — the same number, written two ways, but only one of them is an acceptable final answer. This week fixes any fraction with a radical stuck in the denominator: multiply top and bottom by that same radical — really just multiplying by 1, since $\sqrt{n}/\sqrt{n}=1$ — and the radical always cancels out on the bottom, because $\sqrt{n}\times\sqrt{n}=n$ every time. $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt2}=\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}$; $\dfrac{3}{\sqrt5}=\dfrac{3\sqrt5}{5}$.

Sometimes the leftover radicand still needs simplifying (Lesson 17's skill, right back again): $\dfrac{3}{\sqrt8}=\dfrac{3\sqrt8}{8}=\dfrac{3\sqrt2}{4}$. And adding two radical fractions means rationalizing EACH one first, then finding a common denominator — never the other way around.

## Words to own
- **Rationalize the denominator** — rewrite a fraction so the bottom is a whole number, no radical sign left.
- **The move** — multiply numerator and denominator by the radical in the denominator: $\dfrac{a}{\sqrt{n}}\times\dfrac{\sqrt n}{\sqrt n}=\dfrac{a\sqrt n}{n}$; reduce afterward if the numbers share a factor.
- **Combining** — rationalize both fractions first, then find the common denominator — you can only add once the radicals are gone from the bottom.
- **Conjugate** — the later trick for a TWO-term denominator like $1+\sqrt2$ (multiply by $1-\sqrt2$ instead); a preview, not this week's test.
