# SSS & SAS Proofs

Last week you learned that congruent triangles match in six ways at once. This week hands you the first two shortcuts: the **SSS Postulate** (three matching sides is enough) and the **SAS Postulate** (two matching sides plus the angle *between* them is enough) — and the formal two-column write-up, Statements next to Reasons, that lets you show your work the way a real geometric argument demands. You'll watch a full proof built line by line, then build the same rigidity with your own hands in straws, then write one yourself.

## Words to own
- **SSS Postulate** — three pairs of congruent sides force congruent triangles.
- **SAS Postulate** — two pairs of congruent sides AND their included angle force congruent triangles.
- **Included angle** — the angle formed BETWEEN the two sides you're using, at their shared vertex — not any other angle in the triangle.
- **Two-column proof** — a Given, a Prove, and a Statements | Reasons table, ending with the postulate as the final reason.
- **Reflexive Property** — a shared side or angle is congruent to itself, no measuring required.

## Build-log prompt (bring an answer)
Before Session 1, try this yourself: draw a square ABCD and its diagonal BD, splitting it into $\triangle ABD$ and $\triangle CDB$. Using only "all four sides of a square are congruent" and the fact that BD is the SAME segment in both triangles, argue — in your own words, even roughly — why those two triangles have to be congruent. Write your argument in your Compass. You'll compare it to the formal two-column version in Session 1.
