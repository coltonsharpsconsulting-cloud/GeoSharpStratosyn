# Classifying Triangles

Look at a slice of pizza, a roof truss, and a yield sign — three triangles, three very different jobs, and every one of them can be sorted two separate ways: by its **sides** (are any of them the same length?) and by its **angles** (is any one of them 90° or more?). A triangle always gets one label from each system at once — a roof truss is often isosceles and acute; a yield sign is usually equilateral, which means it's always acute too.

But not every three lengths agree to become a triangle in the first place. Try to build one from a 5-inch, a 7-inch, and a 15-inch stick and the two short ones will never reach far enough to meet the long one — no matter how you tilt them. This week you learn exactly which sets of lengths work, and why.

## Words to own
- **Scalene** — no two sides congruent. **Isosceles** — at least two sides congruent. **Equilateral** — all three sides congruent.
- **Acute** — all three angles under 90°. **Right** — exactly one angle equal to 90°. **Obtuse** — exactly one angle over 90°. **Equiangular** — all three angles congruent (always 60° each).
- **Triangle inequality** — the two shorter sides must add up to MORE than the longest side, or the three lengths can't close into a triangle at all.

## Build-log prompt (bring an answer)
Find three real sticks, straws, or strips of paper of different lengths around your house. Measure them, then use the triangle inequality to **predict** whether they'd close into a triangle — before you actually try it. Sketch your prediction and the real result in your Compass, and note whether they matched.
