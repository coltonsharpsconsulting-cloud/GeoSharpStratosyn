# Circumference & Area of Circles

Trace a coin, a lid, a plate. Each one has a distance around it and a distance across it — and no matter the size, dividing the first by the second always lands on the same number: π.

Two formulas carry the whole lesson. **Circumference** — the distance around — is $C = \pi d = 2\pi r$. **Area** — the space inside — is $A = \pi r^2$. Math-U-See uses **π ≈ 3.14** as a decimal, and **π ≈ 22/7** as a fraction whenever the radius or diameter is a multiple of 7. An **ellipse** (a stretched circle) gets one more formula: multiply one-half its long axis by one-half its short axis by π.

## Words to own
- **Radius (r)** — center to edge. **Diameter (d)** — straight across through the center, $d = 2r$.
- **π (pi)** — the constant ratio $C \div d$, the same for every circle regardless of size.
- **Ellipse** — $A = \pi a b$, where $a$ and $b$ are half the long and short axes.
- **Latitude & longitude** — circles wrapped around the globe: latitude rings run parallel to the equator (north/south); longitude lines pass through the poles from the prime meridian at Greenwich (east/west).

## Build-log prompt (bring an answer)
Find **one circular object at home** — a can lid, a plate, a bike wheel, a clock face. Measure its diameter with a ruler, then wrap a string around it and measure the string. Divide circumference by diameter. How close did you land to 3.14?
