# Transversals

Picture two parallel curb lines at a crosswalk, and the white crosswalk stripes cutting straight across both of them. Each stripe is a **transversal** — a single line slicing through two others — and at each of its two crossings it makes four new angles, eight in all. Most of the time eight angles scattered across two points could measure anything. But because the curb lines are parallel, several of those angles are locked together, guaranteed to match, before anyone ever measures them.

This week you stop looking at one crossing and start looking at *two crossings at once* — and you'll meet the promise that ties them together, plus the promise running backward.

## Words to own
- **Transversal** — a line that crosses two other lines, each at a different point.
- **Interior angles** — the four angles boxed in *between* the two lines. **Exterior angles** — the four *outside* them.
- **Corresponding angles** — same corner at each crossing. **Always congruent** when the lines are parallel.
- **Alternate interior / alternate exterior angles** — interior (or exterior) angles on opposite sides of the transversal. **Always congruent** when the lines are parallel.
- **Converse** — run the promise backward: congruent angles force the lines to be parallel, with no ruler ever laid between them.

## Build-log prompt (bring an answer)

Sketch it in your Compass, number the eight angles it makes (four at each crossing), and circle one pair you think has to measure the same without you checking.
