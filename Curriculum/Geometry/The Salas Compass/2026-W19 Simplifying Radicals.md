# Simplifying Radicals

$\sqrt{49}=7$ exactly — no mess. But $\sqrt{72}$ isn't a whole number, and a calculator only gives you a rounded decimal that never quite ends. This week you learn the exact, smaller way to write it: pull the largest **perfect square** out from under the radical sign, and what's left behind is the simplest form there is — $\sqrt{72} = \sqrt{36 \times 2} = 6\sqrt{2}$.

The same idea decides whether two radical terms can be added at all. $3\sqrt{2}+5\sqrt{2}=8\sqrt{2}$ combines fine — same radicand. But $5\sqrt{2}+3\sqrt{5}$ can never combine into one term, no matter how you simplify — different radicands, forever unlike. And multiplying is just numbers-with-numbers, radicals-with-radicals: $(5\sqrt{3})(6\sqrt{5})=30\sqrt{15}$.

## Words to own
- **Radicand** — the number under the radical sign. **Perfect square** — a whole number whose square root is also a whole number (1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49…).
- **Simplify a radical** — factor out the LARGEST perfect square dividing the radicand, then $\sqrt{a \times b} = \sqrt{a}\times\sqrt{b}$.
- **Like radicals** — the exact same radicand once fully simplified; only like radicals can be added or subtracted (combine the coefficients, keep the radical).
- **Multiplying radicals** — numbers times numbers, radicals times radicals: $\sqrt{a}\times\sqrt{b}=\sqrt{ab}$; sometimes the radical vanishes entirely, since $\sqrt{a}\times\sqrt{a}=a$.

## Build-log prompt (bring an answer)
Pick any whole number between 20 and 100 that is NOT already a perfect square. Simplify its square root by hand — name the largest perfect square you pulled out and what was left underneath. Sketch the split (perfect-square piece + remainder) in your Compass.
