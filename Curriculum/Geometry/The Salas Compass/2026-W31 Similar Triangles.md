# Similar Triangles

For four straight lessons — 23 through 26 — every triangle promise you made was about SAME SIZE: six-for-six congruence, right down to the exact length of every side. This week loosens that grip. Two triangles can be wildly different sizes and still be **similar** — same shape, corresponding angles still congruent, but the sides only have to stay in one constant ratio instead of matching exactly.

The postulate that unlocks it is almost suspiciously easy: match just TWO angles, and the triangles are guaranteed similar. No side ever has to be checked, because a triangle's three angles always add up to 180° — match two, and the third is forced to match too.

## Words to own
- **Similar** — same shape, not necessarily the same size. Corresponding angles congruent; corresponding sides proportional.
- **AA (Angle-Angle) Similarity Postulate** — two congruent angle pairs is enough to prove two triangles similar.
- **Scale factor** — the constant ratio between a pair of corresponding sides in similar figures.
- **Proportion** — an equation of two equal ratios; cross-multiply it to find a missing side.

## Build-log prompt (bring an answer)
Before Session 1, try this yourself: find something with a shadow — a mailbox, a fence post, a person. Guess (don't measure yet) whether a much taller object's shadow, cast at the exact same moment, would let you figure out that object's height using nothing but a ruler and a proportion. Sketch what the two triangles (object + shadow, in each case) would look like, and jot your guess in your Compass. You'll test it for real in Session 2.
